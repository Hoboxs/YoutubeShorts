Was I wrong to set a grocery budget for the dinners my son’s friend is making?

My son, who’s 23, moved back home to our small town after finishing a training program and starting a new job nearby. He’s been close friends with a young woman named Lila (21), and although they weren’t officially dating, they’d been talking daily while she lived several provinces away. Earlier this fall, she left her college program and was staying with a relative, but when that relative told her she needed to move out, she had nowhere to go.

My son plans to move into his own apartment in February and had already said she could live with him then while she looks for work. But she needed a place much sooner, so they both asked if she could stay with us for a couple of months. I said yes. I don’t charge my kids rent, so it felt unfair to charge her either. All I asked was that they be considerate and help keep shared spaces clean. Lila moved in right after Halloween, and honestly, she’s been kind and easy to live with. She’s still applying for jobs, but she’s tried to help out by cooking dinner most nights. I told her she didn’t need to, but she insisted and she’s genuinely a great cook.

About a week in, she wanted to make a specific dish but we didn’t have the ingredients. I offered her my debit card so she could grab what she needed. That turned into her routinely doing the grocery runs, which I didn’t mind at first because she also picked up the basics for the house.

Then the trips became more frequent—three, sometimes four times a week—and she started buying things I don’t normally include in my grocery budget, like specialty ingredients, fresh seafood, and pricier cuts of meat. With six people in the house, that adds up fast. On Sunday, when she left for another grocery run, I asked her to please make sure she gets everything for the week so we’re not constantly buying extra supplies. She said she would.

The next night after dinner, she told my son she wanted to make a fancy roast later in the week but would “need to go back to the store.” My son immediately turned to me and said, “Can you give her your card?” I told them no. I explained I had already paid for the week’s groceries and asked her to plan ahead. I added that a dish like that is too expensive right now, but maybe we could make something similar for a holiday meal. I also said that if she wants to keep cooking—which I appreciate—I’ll need to put a spending limit in place. I told her I wished I’d mentioned it earlier.

Lila looked embarrassed, and my son looked annoyed. Later he told me I made her feel unwelcome when she was “just trying to do something thoughtful.” I said I appreciated the gesture, but she’s buying everything with my money, and I simply can’t afford multiple splurges each week. I also reminded him that he’s welcome to give her money if this means so much to him. He said they’re only here “for a few more weeks” and asked why I couldn’t “just make our guest happy.”

I’m honestly not sure if I handled it poorly or if I’m just protecting my budget.
