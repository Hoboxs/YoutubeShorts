Am I selfish for choosing my career over becoming my brother’s full-time carer?

I’m a 23-year-old woman who just finished university and finally landed a competitive entry-level job in marketing with a major tech company, the kind of opportunity that only comes once if you’re lucky. My younger brother, “Evan,” is 19 and has a genetic disability; he’s bright, affectionate, loves comic books, and can hold conversations, but he also needs daily supervision, help with routines, and careful management of a heart condition. Growing up, I was the default caregiver. From my early teens onward, I handled pickups, medications, meltdowns, bedtime routines, and crisis moments while my parents worked and my older sister moved out and focused on her own life. I missed social events, clubs, and opportunities because I was constantly reminded that Evan trusted me most and “needed” me more than anyone else. I did it without complaint because I love him.

Right as I was preparing to start my new job, my brother’s day program lost funding and cut hours, leaving a gap in his care. At a family lunch, my parents blindsided me and told me they expected me to quit—or at least indefinitely defer—my job to stay home with Evan during the day. They framed it as a temporary sacrifice and a moral obligation, insisting that strangers couldn’t be trusted and that my career could wait. When I asked why my sister couldn’t help or why they couldn’t adjust work schedules or use savings, I was told she had her own life now and that it was “my turn” to step up, even though I already had for most of my childhood. When I refused, my father accused me of choosing money over my disabled brother, and my mother cried that I was abandoning the family. That night, the argument escalated so badly that I packed a bag and moved in with a friend. Since then, relatives have been calling me selfish, saying a few months won’t hurt my career but losing consistent care could devastate my brother. I feel crushed with guilt because I love him deeply, but I also feel like I’ve already given up years of my life and this job represents my first real chance at independence. I can’t tell if I’m finally setting a healthy boundary—or if I’m failing my family when they need me most.
