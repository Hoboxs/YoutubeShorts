Did I go too far banning family after they erased my wife’s past?

After my partner and I returned from our first short trip since our newborn twins arrived, we unlocked our home and immediately realized something was wrong: the study was stripped bare, and the two hand-carved hardwood pieces my partner had inherited from her late grandfather—objects she treasured more than anything in the house—were simply gone. Her aunt, whom we’d trusted with a spare key only for emergencies, stood there beaming and announced she’d done us a “favor” by donating the “old, dusty stuff” and replacing it with cheap, boxed furniture to give the room a “fresh start,” as if she hadn’t just erased a piece of my partner’s history without permission. My partner broke down on the spot, and I told her aunt to leave, demanded the key back, and changed the locks the next day. Now the extended family is flooding us with messages calling me controlling and cruel, accusing me of isolating my partner from her family and punishing a grandmother over “just furniture,” insisting her intentions were pure, while my partner is still crying over something that can never be replaced. She agrees with the ban, but the pressure and guilt are relentless, and I’m left wondering if protecting my partner and our home makes me heartless—or if this boundary was long overdue.
