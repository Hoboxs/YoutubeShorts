Am I wrong for refusing to financially support my parents’ luxury retirement plan?

I’m a 35-year-old man working in healthcare, and I’m an only child. My parents are both in their late 60s and dealing with serious health issues. My mother has a slow-progressing illness, and my father is in the early stages of cognitive decline. Right now, they’re still living independently with some help, but they’ve started planning for the next phase of their lives.

They’ve become very focused on moving into a high-end senior residence in one of the most upscale neighborhoods in our city. The place costs roughly $10,000 a month for a one-bedroom unit, and that only covers basic living, meals, activities, and light housekeeping. Any additional care would cost significantly more.

Financially, they have a steady monthly income that covers their current lifestyle and some savings set aside, but realistically, they wouldn’t be able to sustain living in a place like this long-term, especially once their care needs increase.

The expectation, although not directly stated, is that I would step in and help cover the costs when their money runs out. While I could technically afford to contribute, it would mean taking on a long-term and potentially increasing financial responsibility that could impact my own future plans, including where I live, my career flexibility, and starting a family.

I’ve suggested other senior living options that are still comfortable and well-rated but more financially sustainable. However, those have been dismissed as not good enough. My mother, in particular, tends to focus on what she doesn’t like about options, even when they are objectively good.

From my perspective, this feels like choosing a lifestyle that exceeds what they can reasonably afford, with the assumption that I’ll make up the difference later. I’m not trying to abandon them, I want them to be safe, comfortable, and well cared for. I just don’t think it’s fair for me to be responsible for funding a luxury arrangement long-term, especially when there are solid alternatives.

At the same time, I feel guilty. They’re going through difficult health challenges, and I’m in a position where I could help. Part of me wonders if I should just want them to have the easiest and nicest setup possible.

But another part of me feels like it’s reasonable to expect them to choose something sustainable on their own resources, rather than relying on me for an open-ended commitment.

Am I wrong for setting that boundary?
