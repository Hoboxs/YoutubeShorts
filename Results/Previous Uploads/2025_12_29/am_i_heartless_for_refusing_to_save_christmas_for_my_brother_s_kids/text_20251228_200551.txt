Am I Heartless for Refusing to Save Christmas for My Brother’s Kids?

I’m a woman in my early forties, and my relationship with my younger brother, Evan, has always been distant rather than openly hostile. We didn’t have a dramatic falling-out; we just grew into completely different lives. We live in different countries now, rarely talk outside of obligatory holiday check-ins, and I haven’t seen him, his wife Marina, or their children in person in almost ten years. I don’t have kids, I don’t celebrate Christmas in a traditional way, and my life looks nothing like theirs. Evan, on the other hand, always wanted a loud, chaotic household, and he got it. Between him and Marina, there are eight children, ranging from toddlers to teenagers, some of whom I genuinely wouldn’t recognize if I passed them on the street.

Years ago, once the number of kids passed three, I made a deliberate choice about Christmas gifts. Instead of trying to keep up with individual presents, I started sending one large shared gift for the whole family. Some years it was a big board-game bundle, other years it was a shared entertainment item. My reasoning was simple: one meaningful, higher-quality gift felt more sincere than sending a pile of cheap items that would be forgotten by New Year’s. Evan never complained outright, so I assumed it was fine.

That changed last week. Evan called me out of the blue, sounding frantic and exhausted. He explained that money was tighter than ever this year and said the kids were already anxious about Christmas. Then he dropped the bombshell: he wanted me to buy individual gifts for each child, so they would all have something to unwrap. Wanting to at least consider it, I asked him to send me their wish lists.

When I opened the email, my stomach dropped. Nearly every child had requested expensive electronics, specialty gaming gear, or brand-name items that were well over $120 each. Even choosing the cheapest option per child would have pushed the total close to $1,400. I stared at the screen in disbelief. I’m not wealthy, I’m not close to these kids, and I don’t even participate in Christmas traditions myself. The idea that I was suddenly expected to bankroll an entire holiday felt unreal.

I called Evan back and tried to be gentle but firm. I told him I understood they were struggling, but I simply could not afford to spend that kind of money. I suggested cheaper alternatives or sticking with the shared-gift tradition. That’s when everything blew up. He accused me of being cold, said I didn’t understand what it’s like to see your kids disappointed on Christmas morning, and implied that because I don’t have children, I “hoard” my money. When I repeated that my finances weren’t limitless, he snapped, called me selfish, and hung up on me.

We haven’t spoken since. Now I’m sitting with this heavy mix of guilt, anger, and disbelief. I feel for his situation, but I also feel like I’ve been reduced to a distant relative whose only role is to cover what they can’t afford. I can’t shake the feeling that no matter what I give, it will never be enough—and that this year, I was expected to sacrifice my own financial stability to keep the peace.

So am I really the villain here for refusing to spend a small fortune to rescue someone else’s Christmas?
