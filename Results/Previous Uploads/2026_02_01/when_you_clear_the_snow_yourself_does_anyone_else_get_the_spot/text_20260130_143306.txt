When You Clear the Snow Yourself, Does Anyone Else Get the Spot?

I am in my late twenties and have lived in the same small mixed use building for several years. Until recently, winter was never an issue here. The previous owner always made sure snow was cleared quickly, so I never even owned a shovel. Last spring, the building was sold, and the new owner has taken a very hands off approach to maintenance. That did not matter much until we were hit with a heavy snowstorm that dumped well over a foot of snow overnight, and days passed with no plowing or clearing at all.

Knowing the snow would eventually freeze solid, I walked to a nearby store, bought a shovel, and spent a long time digging out my car. I also cleared a wide path across part of the parking area so I could actually pull out and leave. There is no officially assigned parking, but everyone who lives there has parked in the same general spots for years, so it is very obvious who usually parks where.

I ran a couple of quick errands and was gone for about an hour. When I came back, another car was parked directly in the space I had cleared. It was technically the only usable spot, but it only existed because of the work I had just done. I had no intention of digging out another place, so I parked behind that car instead. I had an online meeting starting soon and did not feel like going door to door trying to figure out whose guest it was, especially since there are only a few residential units and the businesses downstairs were closed.

While I was in the meeting, someone started banging on my door. I ignored it at first, but they kept coming back every few minutes. Once the meeting ended, I opened the door to find an extremely angry woman demanding that I move my car immediately and yelling about how long she had been stuck. I told her I had cleared that spot myself and had nowhere else to park.

As I went outside to move my car, one of my neighbors joined in. He said I should have asked around to coordinate when people needed to leave so we could decide who parked behind whom. I was confused by that logic, since he could have cleared a spot himself if he knew he was having a visitor. He pointed out that parking was not assigned and said he did not even own a shovel.

The argument dragged on, and eventually everyone went their separate ways. Before leaving, my neighbor told me he used to think I was quiet and respectful, but now he claimed he had seen my true character. I did not feel like I had done anything unreasonable, but now there is obvious tension where there was none before.

I still feel like I was justified, but I cannot help wondering if standing my ground over a snow cleared parking spot was worth creating bad blood with people I live next to.
