Am I Seriously Expected to Smile at Christmas After My Parent Drained My Entire Future?

I just turned eighteen and live in a northern state where residents receive annual dividend payments from natural resource revenue. Those payments start when you’re a baby and add up over time. Once I became an adult, I did what most people my age would do—I asked what had happened to mine. By my math, even without interest or investments, it should have totaled close to $30,000.

That’s when everything cracked open.

When I asked my parent about the money, she brushed it off at first, saying it went toward “medical expenses and necessities.” That explanation immediately felt wrong. I was double-insured my entire childhood and rarely had out-of-pocket medical bills. When I pushed back, her story shifted. She admitted some of the money was used on household expenses, but insisted she would never spend it on anything that didn’t somehow involve me.

Then things got ugly.

Instead of giving me clear answers, she accused my other parent of “putting ideas in my head” and trying to turn me against her. That wasn’t true. No one influenced me. I was simply asking where my money went—money that could have helped me with school, housing, or just surviving early adulthood. She then told me she struggled financially for years and had no choice but to dip into it.

Here’s the part that shattered whatever trust I had left: according to the divorce agreement between my parents, any money taken from my dividend account was supposed to be repaid. Despite that legal requirement, when I finally learned the balance, there was barely $500 left.

What should have been a safety net for my future is gone.

The conversation spiraled after that—defensiveness, guilt-tripping, emotional blowups. Nothing was resolved. And now, with the holidays coming up, I’m expected to show up for Christmas like everything is normal. Like I didn’t just find out that tens of thousands of dollars legally meant for me vanished without accountability.

I’m sitting here trying to process betrayal, financial loss, and the weight of starting adulthood with nothing—while being told I’m dramatic for being upset and selfish for not wanting to celebrate.

And honestly? I don’t know how I’m supposed to pretend this didn’t happen.
