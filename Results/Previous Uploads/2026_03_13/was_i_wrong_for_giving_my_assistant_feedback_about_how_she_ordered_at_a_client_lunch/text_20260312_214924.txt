Was I wrong for giving my assistant feedback about how she ordered at a client lunch?

Earlier this year I hired a new assistant, I’ll call her Emily. She’s very good at her job, and I’ve already given her a raise because I felt she was underpaid for the level of work she was doing. Recently I even gave her the lead role on one of our larger accounts, and she handled it extremely well.

Because of that, I scheduled a business lunch with the client and invited Emily along. I told the client ahead of time that she had played a big role in the project and that I wanted her included in the meeting. He was completely fine with it.

During the lunch itself, Emily was professional, knowledgeable, and contributed well to the conversation. Both the client and I were impressed overall.

The only awkward moment came when we ordered food. The client and I both ordered simple meals, burgers with fries, and finished around the same time. Emily ordered a larger meal with multiple sides and took quite a bit longer to finish eating. The client and I ended up waiting around for about fifteen minutes after we finished.

Then when the server asked if we wanted dessert, the client and I declined, but Emily ordered cheesecake and coffee.

After the meeting, I realized I had never actually explained the usual etiquette for client lunches. So I spoke with her privately and tried to frame it as professional advice. I told her that during client meals it’s generally best to follow the client’s lead, if they order something simple, we do the same; if they decline dessert, we usually do too. If we’re still hungry afterward, we can always grab something later.

She initially offered to pay me back for the meal, but I clarified that it wasn’t about the cost at all. I’m happy to pay for lunch, the point was more about the business dynamic. I explained that I personally choose foods that are easy to eat quickly and cleanly, and that allow me to keep pace with the client so no one ends up waiting awkwardly.

At that point she became very upset and started crying. She said it was because she’s overweight and assumed I was criticizing her eating because of that. I told her that wasn’t my intention at all and that I was simply sharing professional strategy. I even gave examples of my own past mistakes at business meals, like ordering something messy and spilling it on my shirt, or accidentally ordering something that offended a client’s dietary preferences.

Despite that, she was still upset when she left.

I genuinely meant it as constructive guidance because I think she has a lot of potential and I want to help her succeed. But now I’m wondering if I handled it poorly or if I shouldn’t have brought it up at all.

Was I wrong for mentioning it?
