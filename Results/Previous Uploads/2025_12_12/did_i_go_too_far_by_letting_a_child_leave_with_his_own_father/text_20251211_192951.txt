Did I Go Too Far by Letting a Child Leave With His Own Father?

Last weekend in Maple Ridge, I agreed—at the very last minute—to drive a teammate of my son to their late-afternoon match. My wife had already arranged the ride with the boy’s mother, Lila, who is a single parent and usually swaps driving duties with my wife every Thursday evening. Since my wife was traveling for work, I stepped in without hesitation. I’ve helped before, and every other time, the routine was simple: I’d bring the boy back to our house afterward, and Lila would collect him from there.

But this time unfolded differently. Near the end of the game, Lila’s ex-husband—Marcus—showed up. I knew him casually from years ago, enough to say hello to, and while I’d heard plenty through my wife about how tense their relationship had once been, none of it had ever involved anything that affected me directly. When I saw him chatting with Lila’s mother beside the bleachers, I stepped over and asked, “Are you taking Rowan home tonight?” Marcus nodded and said yes. Lila’s mother didn’t object or express worry; she simply asked me to let her daughter know her phone had run out of power.

So I left the field, assuming all was well.

The moment I walked through our front door, my wife lit into me—furious, shaking, demanding to know why I had “allowed” Marcus to leave with his own son. She said I had handed the boy over to someone she believed was volatile, someone she had long warned me about. She insisted I should have waited, refused the handoff, or at the very least contacted her before leaving. She said I’d put the child at risk without even realizing it.

I was stunned. From my perspective, the boy’s grandmother approved, his father confirmed he was taking him home, and nothing seemed unusual. But my wife maintains that I should have known better and that I overlooked something serious.

Did I genuinely mishandle the situation, or did I simply trust a parent to take his own child when everyone present seemed to agree?
