Should I Have Left Work Early for a Colleague’s Sick Child?

I commute to work each day with a small group of colleagues since we all live far from the office and splitting the long drive helps with fuel and highway costs. One coworker—I'll call her Mara—has a young son who’s been dealing with severe nosebleeds that soak through anything she tries to use. Over the weekend his symptoms got so intense that her family rushed him to urgent care.

The next morning she still showed up at work and didn’t mention anything until we were already driving home. That’s when she explained that she never asked her supervisor for time off because she assumed the answer would be no. She also said that if he didn’t get medical attention soon, it could become life-threatening.

Then she asked all of us to switch to her car for the next day’s commute and leave work almost an hour earlier than our scheduled shift. Two of us finish at the latest time slot, so this wasn’t something we could just do without approval. We told her we needed to check with our supervisors first.

After talking it over, the other late-shift coworker and I agreed that she should simply bring her own car so she could leave whenever she needed, and we’d arrange a different ride home. That way she could rush straight back to her son without waiting for the rest of us. It seemed like the easiest and fastest solution for her.

But when she heard this plan, she got upset. She said we didn’t care about her son, made a pointed comment about people getting what they deserve, and insisted she’d cancel the appointment so she wouldn’t “interrupt our schedule,” even though she was the one who first suggested taking her own car and we never asked her to change anything.

I can’t understand why she said the situation might be life-threatening but then chose to quietly go to work all day, or why she expected the entire carpool to leave early without clearing it beforehand. If I believed my child was in danger, I’d have left work immediately, not waited until a group ride could be arranged.

So now I’m wondering—am I wrong for not clocking out early when we offered a plan that let her leave sooner anyway?
