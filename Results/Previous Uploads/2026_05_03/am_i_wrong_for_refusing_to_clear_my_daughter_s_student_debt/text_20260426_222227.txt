Am I wrong for refusing to clear my daughter’s student debt?

I am struggling with this because part of me feels firm in my decision, and another part keeps wondering if I handled it too harshly. My daughter is twenty six now, and she finished university about three years ago. When she was choosing what to study, we had many long, difficult conversations. I was very clear that I believed she should choose something practical, something that would allow her to support herself comfortably. She, however, had her heart set on performing arts, and no matter what I said, she would not change her mind. It caused a lot of tension between us at the time, and she made it very clear that she was going to follow her passion no matter what I thought.

To be clear, I was not in a position to pay for any of my children’s education. They all had to take out loans to get through school. That was simply our financial reality. I never singled her out in that sense. But I did warn her, more than once, that the path she was choosing could make things financially difficult later on.

Now, years later, she is working in a themed dining venue where the staff perform while serving guests. It is not a high-paying job, and she shares a place with a couple of roommates to make ends meet. Recently, my own mother passed away, and I inherited a large sum of money. It is more than I have ever had at once, and my plan has been to put most of it toward my retirement and use some of it to take the whole family on a trip abroad, something we have never done together.

I was genuinely excited when I told everyone over dinner. I imagined it as a chance for us to come together and enjoy something special after a difficult time. But my daughter was quiet throughout the evening, and later she pulled me aside. She asked me if I would use the inheritance to pay off her student loans instead.

I told her no.

That is when everything escalated. She became upset, and I told her that this situation was the result of her own choices. I reminded her of the conversations we had years ago and said it would not be fair to her siblings if I used such a large amount of money to solve her problem alone. From my perspective, they all took on similar responsibilities, and it would create resentment if I treated her differently.

She did not see it that way at all. She said I was punishing her for following her passion and throwing my past warnings back in her face. She was hurt, angry, and said she would not be joining the family trip at all.

Now I am left wondering if I approached this too rigidly. I still believe that her education and the loans that came with it were her responsibility, and that it would not be fair to the rest of the family to step in now. But at the same time, I can see how this might feel to her, especially given the timing and the emotional weight of everything that has happened recently.

I cannot tell if I am standing by a reasonable principle or if I have let that principle damage my relationship with my daughter in a way that will be difficult to repair.
