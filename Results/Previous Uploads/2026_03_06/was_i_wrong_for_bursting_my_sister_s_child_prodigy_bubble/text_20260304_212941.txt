Was I wrong for bursting my sister’s “child prodigy” bubble?

My older sister, “Marina,” had her first son fourteen months ago, and ever since then she’s been convinced she gave birth to a once in a generation mind. I adore my nephew. He’s sweet, funny, and totally lovable. But according to Marina, he’s also operating on a level far beyond the average toddler.

If he points to his nose, she calls it “advanced body awareness.” If he says a half-clear word, she swears most kids his age can’t communicate that well. When he figured out how to push a toy car and make the wheels spin, she announced he “clearly understands mechanics.” She frequently mentions how her doctor is “extremely impressed,” which sounds to me like standard polite feedback every parent hears.

What makes it harder is the subtle comparison. At family lunches she’ll turn to me or our cousins and ask, “When did your kids start doing this?” or “Be honest, that’s early, right?” It’s technically phrased as curiosity, but it feels like she’s waiting for confirmation that her son is ahead.

Last weekend at our parents’ house, she pulled out a board book and showed us how he could point to different animals when prompted. She started saying she wouldn’t be surprised if he tested as gifted one day because of his “pattern recognition.” I chuckled and said something like, “He’s a perfectly typical toddler. You just think everything he does is groundbreaking because you’re his mom.”

The mood shifted instantly. She went quiet, then told me I was being cynical and unsupportive. Later she messaged me saying I made her feel foolish in front of everyone and that, as her sister, I should celebrate her excitement instead of knocking it down.

I apologized and told her I didn’t mean to embarrass her. But if I’m honest, the constant framing of normal milestones as extraordinary was starting to grate on me. I wasn’t attacking her son, I was reacting to what feels like nonstop bragging wrapped in questions.

She says we’re fine now, but I can still sense some distance.

Was I wrong for saying what I did, even if I felt it needed to be said?
