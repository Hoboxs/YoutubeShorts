Do I Owe Half My Life’s Success to the Brother Who Never Left?

I grew up as the overlooked middle child in a struggling rural family where favoritism was obvious and opportunity was scarce. When a chance came for one son to work abroad, my mother pushed me out the door—not because she believed in me, but because she wanted my older brother close to her. I left behind my newly married wife, missed the birth of my child, and spent years grinding through exhausting work in a foreign country, sending nearly everything I earned back home just to keep the family afloat.

While I was gone, I lived in constant guilt and loneliness, telling myself the sacrifice would mean something one day. After years of this, I took a risk no one supported. I sold part of the land that legally belonged to me and poured everything into starting a business. My family mocked the idea and warned I was throwing my life away. Instead, the business succeeded beyond anything I imagined. I finally moved my wife and child to the city, bought a home, and gave them stability I never had.

That’s when my family turned on me. My mother and siblings claimed my success wasn’t really mine—that because my brother stayed behind, he deserved half of everything I built. They said I only succeeded because he “held things together,” ignoring the fact that I funded the household for years, missed irreplaceable moments with my child, and even allowed him to profit from my land while I was gone. Now my extended family and community call me selfish for refusing to hand over half my future.

After a lifetime of being pushed aside, this is the first thing that has ever truly been mine—and I’m being told I don’t deserve to keep it.
