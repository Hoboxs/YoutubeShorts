Can I Really Sit Front Row at a Wedding Built on Betrayal?

For most of my life, my father, now in his early sixties, was married to my mother, whom he treated like an afterthought for nearly three decades. Their marriage unraveled slowly and painfully, marked by repeated affairs, quiet humiliations, and a kind of disrespect that became normal behind closed doors. When they finally divorced, it felt less like a tragedy and more like an overdue ending to something that had been broken for years. I made my peace with that and even accepted the idea that one day he might remarry.

What I can’t accept is who he’s marrying. The woman he’s engaged to is the last person he cheated on my mother with, the final betrayal before everything collapsed. As if that weren’t surreal enough, she also happens to share my mother’s first name, which makes the whole situation feel like some cruel joke I don’t understand. I’ve never met her, I’ve never wanted to, and I genuinely don’t know how I’d even refer to her without feeling sick to my stomach. My siblings, after their own tense conversations with him, have already made it clear they won’t be attending the ceremony.

I tried to stay quiet and avoid the confrontation, hoping distance would speak for me, but recently my father cornered me and asked if I’d not only attend the wedding, but take part in it. The request felt unreal, like he expected me to celebrate a relationship that exists because my family was torn apart. The idea of standing there, smiling for photos, and pretending this union isn’t built on years of damage feels wrong on a level I can’t explain. Now I’m stuck wondering whether refusing makes me disloyal, or if going would mean betraying myself.
