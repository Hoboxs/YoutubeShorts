Am I Wrong for Wanting to Confront My Brother About How He Feeds His Child?

My niece, who I’ll call Lena, is only seven, and watching her grow up has started to feel less like normal babysitting and more like watching a slow-motion crisis. I help out by watching her a few afternoons a week when my brother Evan and his wife Claire are working, and while Lena is sweet, funny, and incredibly affectionate, her relationship with food is deeply unsettling. She’s already well over 130 pounds and barely taller than my shoulder when I sit down, and she is constantly asking to eat—sometimes minutes after finishing a full meal. Food seems to be the only comfort she’s ever been taught.

At home, her parents have never enforced any boundaries around eating. Vegetables are completely optional, and in practice, nonexistent. Claire likes to joke that “kids know their bodies,” even while laughing about Lena drinking so much milk she throws up. They regularly make separate meals just for her, always processed and fried, while the adults eat something else. I’ve watched her eat an entire family-sized box of syrup-drenched sausage pancakes in one day, followed by dessert, because she cried for it. No one said no. No one ever does.

What makes this worse is that Lena has already started talking about being teased at school. She’s not even in second grade yet, and she knows she’s different. I’ve tried, gently, to offer healthier options when she’s with me, but she refuses everything that isn’t breaded, sweet, or soaked in sauce, and I have no authority to push it further because her parents immediately undo any limits I set. Once, during a family gathering, she ate an entire loaf of garlic bread meant for ten people while my back was turned. When I checked later, that bread alone had more calories than most adults need in a full day.

I’m exhausted, worried, and honestly starting to feel resentful, which makes me feel awful because none of this is her fault. Both of her parents are thin, dismissive, and defensive, and I know bringing this up could explode into a huge fight. But standing by while they laugh it off feels just as wrong. I don’t want to shame them or her—I want to protect a little girl who’s being set up for a lifetime of health issues and cruelty from other kids.

If I finally tell my brother and his wife that their approach to feeding their child is harming her, would that make me the bad guy?
