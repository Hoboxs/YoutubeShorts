Was I wrong for telling a hospital nurse to stop lecturing me?

I have spent the past week helping my mother after she injured her foot badly enough that she can barely get around. I have been driving her to appointments, carrying groceries, helping around the house, basically doing everything I can to make things easier for her while she recovers. She is still fairly young, but after several exhausting days, she was already worn down before we even arrived at the emergency room.

The hospital was absolute chaos from the moment we walked in.

There were no wheelchairs available anywhere in the emergency department, so my mother was struggling to balance on crutches while standing in long lines. She was visibly exhausted and in pain. During triage, the nurse told me to go search the building for a wheelchair because they apparently had none left nearby.

I started walking off but quickly realized I had no idea where I was supposed to go. I returned to ask for clarification, and a paramedic nearby mentioned she did not think there were any available at the front entrance either. Before she could even finish, the triage nurse cut her off sharply and said something along the lines of, “She does not know what she is talking about. I work here, not her.”

The paramedic looked irritated by that comment but kindly offered to walk me to the main entrance anyway because I had never been to that hospital before and clearly looked lost.

When we got there, there was a row of wheelchairs. The paramedic grabbed one, and I grabbed another from the exact same area. The only difference was that mine felt slightly damp. Since I had recently been at another hospital where staff regularly sanitized wheelchairs before stacking them, I assumed it had simply been cleaned moments earlier.

So I brought it back.

The second I rolled it into the emergency room, the triage nurse suddenly started speaking to me like I was some irresponsible teenager. She launched into this long lecture about being inconsiderate, about how people need to take better care of elderly patients, and about how I needed to “do better.” The way she spoke made it sound like I had deliberately done something cruel to my own mother.

I was honestly stunned.

My mother is not even elderly. She is in her fifties, fully alert, and standing right there. I had spent the entire week helping her nonstop, and now I was being treated like I had neglected her because I unknowingly grabbed a wheelchair that apparently was not clean enough.

I never yelled. I never insulted her. I simply said calmly, “Please stop scolding me.”

That somehow made things even worse.

She immediately started criticizing me for saying that and continued lecturing me about respecting older people and needing to behave better. At that point I felt trapped in this bizarre conversation where every attempt to politely end it only encouraged her to continue. I finally had to repeat several times that I just wanted her to stop.

Eventually she did, but the whole interaction left me shaken and embarrassed.

My mother later told me I probably should have stayed quiet because arguing with hospital staff never helps anything. But honestly, I do not even feel like I argued. I felt like I was trying to defend myself from being publicly talked down to while already stressed and overwhelmed.

Now I keep replaying the situation in my head wondering if I should have just apologized and moved on, or whether it was reasonable to ask someone to stop speaking to me like I was a horrible person over an honest mistake.
