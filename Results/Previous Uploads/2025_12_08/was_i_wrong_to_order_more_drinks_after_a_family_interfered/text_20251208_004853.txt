Was I Wrong to Order More Drinks After a Family Interfered?

During a short solo holiday in Malta this spring, I booked an all-inclusive resort so I wouldn’t have to wander around alone looking for places to eat. The dining hall had communal tables that sat about eight people each, which the staff said helped encourage conversation among travelers.

One evening, I arrived early and was enjoying a glass of rosé when a group of four—a couple and their two young nieces—were placed at the same table. As soon as they sat down, the woman leaned over and told me they didn’t want the children “exposed to adults drinking.” I politely suggested they could ask to be moved if it made them uncomfortable, but I planned to finish my drink. When they noticed I was traveling alone, they exchanged comments meant to sound sympathetic but clearly weren’t.

When I went to get food, my glass vanished. A server approached and asked to check my wristband because the family claimed I was a minor dining without supervision. He realized quickly what had happened, apologized, and brought me a fresh drink just as the family returned with their plates.

Feeling both amused and irritated at how far they had taken things, I asked the server if he could bring the full bottle along with two small liqueur pours. He did, and I calmly took the drinks as he set them down. The family stood up immediately, gathered their things without a word, and left the dining hall in a rush.

Now I’m wondering whether ordering the extra drinks was excessive—or simply a reasonable response after being treated like a problem at my own table.
