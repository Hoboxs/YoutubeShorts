Was I Wrong for Saying Something When One Person Took Almost Everything?

My partner and I went to a large Asian-style buffet to ring in the new year together, hoping for a relaxed, festive meal. As we walked along the hot food line, we noticed one of the most popular items—sticky glazed pork ribs—had just run out, but a server was weaving through the crowd with a fresh metal tray. We stepped aside politely and waited, assuming it would be fair game once it was set down.

The moment the tray hit the counter, another diner lunged for the serving tongs and began piling ribs onto her plate. At first, I didn’t think much of it, figuring she’d take a portion and move on. Instead, she kept going, carefully stacking piece after piece until her plate was nearly overflowing. Then, to my surprise, she grabbed a second plate and waved over a young boy—possibly her grandson—and started loading that one too. By the time she was done, close to half the tray was gone, and a small crowd had formed behind us waiting.

Under my breath, but loud enough to carry, I remarked to my partner something like, “Wow, I guess everyone else is just supposed to miss out.” The woman clearly heard me. She spun around, slammed the tongs back into the tray with a sharp clatter, shot us an angry look, and stormed off without taking another bite.

My partner immediately looked mortified and told me I’d made a scene for no reason. She said it would’ve been easier—and less awkward—to just grab something else and circle back later instead of calling attention to it, but was I really wrong for speaking up when one person was clearly taking more than their share?
