Is It Wrong to Cut Someone Off After They Steal a Symbol of Your Recovery?

I’m a woman in my mid-thirties living in a large Rust Belt city, and one of the ways I’ve stayed anchored since getting sober has been collecting high-end perfumes, with certain bottles marking major milestones in my recovery. A few years ago, I became close with a woman I met through a sobriety support group, and we bonded over fragrance instead of alcohol, talking endlessly about brands like Byredo, Maison Francis Kurkdjian, and Diptyque. Over time, she started asking if I could decant small amounts of my perfumes for her, and most of the time I didn’t mind at all, since fragrance expires and I had more than I could ever reasonably use. The one exception was a bottle of Maison Margiela Replica “Jazz Club,” which I bought to celebrate my first full year sober and which, for me, is inseparable from surviving withdrawal, rebuilding my life, and learning how to exist without alcohol. When she asked for that one, I gently declined and suggested she grab a free sample from Sephora or Nordstrom, which somehow turned into accusations that I was being controlling, elitist, and afraid of anyone else smelling like me, despite the fact that I’d shared nearly every other scent I owned. She eventually apologized and I accepted, but after that I began noticing the warm, smoky scent lingering in my bedroom after she visited, and later unmistakably on her clothes when we met, even though she insisted it came from a sample. I slowly distanced myself, but months later, during a crowded autumn gathering at my apartment, she disappeared into the bathroom for an unusually long time, and when I followed the smell, I realized my “Jazz Club” bottle was gone from my dresser. What followed was a public, humiliating confrontation that ended with her admitting she’d been secretly filling travel atomizers from my bottle because she was stressed, struggling, and found the scent comforting, minimizing it as “just perfume” while the bottle in her bag was visibly emptier. Now our mutual friends keep urging me to forgive her, framing it as a trauma-driven lapse tied to addiction and insisting I’m being harsh if I don’t let it go, but I can’t shake the feeling that she chose that specific bottle precisely because of what it meant to me, leaving me wondering whether standing my ground makes me heartless or whether forgiveness stops being automatic when someone knowingly steals a symbol of your survival?
