Did I ruin our Christmas game by guessing the word too fast?

After Christmas dinner, when the lights were low, the tree was still glowing, and everyone was settled in that warm, overstuffed, slightly chaotic holiday mood, my extended family decided to play our usual Christmas guessing game. It’s something we do almost every year. Everyone writes a Christmas-related word or phrase on a slip of paper — songs, traditions, characters, folklore — tosses them into a bowl, and then we randomly tape one to someone else’s forehead. The goal isn’t really to win; it’s to drag it out as long as possible. People ask ridiculous yes-or-no questions, misunderstand the rules, argue over technicalities, and the whole thing usually dissolves into laughter.

For reference, earlier in the game I had written “The Nice List,” which my uncle ended up guessing after a long, goofy struggle. So everything was normal… until it was my turn.

As soon as the paper went on my forehead, things felt off. One cousin squinted at me and said, genuinely confused, “I don’t even know what that is.” Another person shrugged and said they’d “heard of it but didn’t really get it.” That immediately set off alarm bells in my head — because this wasn’t supposed to be an obscure trivia contest. Then it clicked.

My brother is obsessed with obscure Christmas lore. Not Santa, not reindeer, not Hallmark stuff — he’s into the dark, old European folklore. Every year he brings up weird traditions no one asked about. And there’s one character he talks about constantly.

So I said, half-joking, “I think I know whose word this is.” He didn’t deny it.

I asked my first question:
“Am I something you can hold in your hands?”

Everyone said no. My uncle added, “I still don’t know what that is.”

That was enough.

I didn’t even need a second question. I immediately said:
“Am I the Krampus?”

I was right.

The game ended instantly — I’d barely started, and the round was over before anyone could stall, joke, or argue about technicalities. Instead of laughter, there was a long pause… and then my brother got genuinely upset. He said I’d ruined the fun, that he’d been excited to watch people struggle to figure out Krampus, and that I “took the fun out of it” by guessing so fast. He made it clear he felt like I’d spoiled a Christmas moment he was looking forward to.

Now I’m stuck wondering if I actually Grinched the game by winning too efficiently, or if it’s unreasonable to expect someone not to connect the dots when the answer is an obscure Christmas demon only one person in the room is obsessed with.

So did I ruin Christmas game night, or was this just an unfortunate case of knowing your family too well?
