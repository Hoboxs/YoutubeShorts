Was I Wrong to Let My Kid Keep a Rare Collectible After a Fair Swap?

During the holidays, a simple gift exchange turned into unexpected family tension. My daughter, Elara (11), and her younger cousin, Brielle (7), were each given a blind-box collectible called a MoonMallow Sprite, a popular vinyl toy similar to Labubu where some designs are produced in smaller numbers. When they opened their boxes, Brielle received a beige Forest Sprite, while Elara opened a coral Star Sprite. Brielle immediately became upset because she wanted the coral one, and Elara—who already owned that version—offered to trade because she liked the beige design better. The swap happened quickly, no adults intervened, and both kids were happy afterward.

A day later, Brielle’s parents found out through an online group that the beige Forest Sprite was a “secret run” figure and could resell for around $190. Once they learned that, they contacted me and demanded the kids switch toys back, even asking me to drive nearly an hour late at night to return it. When I asked if Brielle actually wanted the beige figure again, they said that wasn’t relevant. After more discussion, her father admitted someone had already offered him $170 for it, and he planned to sell it and then buy Brielle a different coral one.

I refused to force my daughter to give it back. From my perspective, the trade was fair at the time: both children agreed, Elara didn’t pressure Brielle, and while Elara knew the Forest Sprite was harder to pull, she didn’t know it had resale value or that it could be sold for real money. Brielle originally didn’t want that figure at all and only changed her parents’ minds once money entered the picture.

Now Brielle’s parents are upset, accusing me of taking advantage of a child, and one of them even said something extremely harsh about my daughter that I felt crossed a line. The relative who gave the toys says this is unnecessary adult drama and has stepped away from the situation, leaving the conflict unresolved, so was I wrong for standing firm and letting my daughter keep the toy she fairly traded for?
