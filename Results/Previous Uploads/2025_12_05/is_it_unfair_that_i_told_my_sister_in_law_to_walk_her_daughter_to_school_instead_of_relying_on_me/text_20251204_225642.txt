Is it unfair that I told my sister-in-law to walk her daughter to school instead of relying on me?

I’m in my mid-20s, working irregular hours in healthcare while living with my mom. She owns the house outright, and I pay her reduced rent and handle most of the chores because our schedules are chaotic. She’s self-employed and often travels.

Last week, my older sister “Lena,” her husband “Evan,” and their 7-year-old daughter moved in temporarily. Their house turned out to have major plumbing and heating issues that will take weeks to repair, so staying with us made the most sense.

A day after they arrived, Lena asked if I could take their daughter to school every morning. The school is a short walk from our mom’s house—15 minutes, flat sidewalks, crosswalks, everything. But Lena and Evan normally rely on a neighbor for drop-offs, and Evan’s job starts very early. They share one car, and Lena only works one weekend shift, so she’s usually home.

I told her I couldn’t commit to doing morning school runs because my own shifts are all over the place. Sometimes I get off at 9 or 10 a.m. after an overnight shift, and other times I’m clocking in before sunrise. There is no world where my boss would accept me saying, “Sorry, I need my mornings free for school drop-off,” without cutting my hours, which I can’t afford.

Instead of discussing it, Lena got emotional and said she needed me to do this, that it would “just make things easier.” When I gently pointed out that she could simply walk her daughter to school—since she’s home most mornings—she said that a 15-minute walk was “too long for a young kid.” I even pulled up the distance on my phone, thinking she hadn’t realized how close it was, but she doubled down.

I told her I couldn’t take on daily drop-offs and that walking really was the simplest solution.

Later, my mom told me Lena had gone to her crying about it. Mom said it would be a kind thing for me to help, but she also acknowledged that my schedule is unpredictable and didn’t push further.

Now Evan is calling me selfish, insisting it’s a “small ask” since their old neighbor could do it. But their old neighbor also didn’t work rotating shifts and wasn’t coming off 10-hour nights.

I feel like I’m losing my mind, because when I went to that same school, my mom literally walked me every morning from the same house.

So was I really in the wrong for telling Lena she needs to walk her daughter instead of expecting me to rearrange my work life?
