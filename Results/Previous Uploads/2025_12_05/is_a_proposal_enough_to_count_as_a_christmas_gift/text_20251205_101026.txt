Is a Proposal Enough to Count as a Christmas Gift?

I have a friend, let's call him Nathan, who I've known for over a decade. Nathan has always been a solid friend, and he was even part of my own wedding celebration. He's been dating his girlfriend, Emma, for around six years, and she truly brings out the best in him. A few weeks ago, Nathan confided in me that he was planning to propose to Emma over the holidays. He’s arranging for his parents to “unexpectedly” visit them early one morning while they’re spending Christmas with her family, and he wants to pop the question during the gift opening, with both families present.

When we were chatting about the plans, Nathan casually mentioned that he hadn’t bought any other Christmas presents for Emma because he was counting the engagement as her gift. I told him that getting engaged and exchanging gifts don’t have to be mutually exclusive. When I got engaged—even though it wasn’t during a holiday—I still received other thoughtful gifts. Nathan argued that the ring alone was more expensive than the usual combined gifts he might get her, which is true, but he wasn’t stretching his budget to the limit and could reasonably get a smaller gift as well. I also noted that since he wanted the proposal to be a surprise, not having any other gifts would likely make Emma suspicious.

We debated this a bit more. Nathan insisted that “getting engaged” counts as a gift, but I explained that Emma will likely still buy him Christmas presents she knows he’ll enjoy, and the engagement isn’t the same type of exchange. She’s not materialistic, but she does appreciate the tradition of exchanging gifts and the thought behind them. Nathan seemed frustrated by my perspective and mentioned he probably won’t share further details with me because of our “differing views.” I don’t want to push the point too hard, but I’m curious whether my advice really was off base.
