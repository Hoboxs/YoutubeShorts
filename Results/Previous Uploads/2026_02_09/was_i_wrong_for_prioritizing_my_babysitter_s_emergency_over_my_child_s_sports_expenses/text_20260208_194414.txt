Was I wrong for prioritizing my babysitter’s emergency over my child’s sports expenses?

I’m a single mother raising three children on my own: a nine-year-old son, a five-year-old daughter, and a three-year-old toddler. During the workweek, I rely on a babysitter named Riley, who picks the kids up from school and daycare and stays with them until I get home. She usually works somewhere between twelve and fifteen hours a week and has been reliable and great with the kids.

One important detail is that Riley has a severe allergy to tree nuts. We’ve always taken that seriously in my home. We keep an EpiPen available for her, and the only product containing tree nuts we ever have is a chocolate spread that’s strictly reserved for weekends when she isn’t working. Everyone involved is aware of this, including my ex.

One weekend, the kids were at their father’s place. When they came back, he had packed their lunches with a trail mix that apparently contained nuts. During the week, while Riley was unpacking their lunchboxes, she had an allergic reaction. She tried calling me, but I’m not allowed to have my phone on me at work. She then called our emergency contacts, including my neighbor and my partner, but no one answered.

At that point, Riley stayed calm, packed up a bag with snacks, toys, and her medication, loaded all three kids into her car, and drove herself and them to the emergency room. Thankfully, everything turned out okay. By the time I arrived, she had been treated, the kids were fed, distracted, and safe, and the situation was under control, though understandably stressful for everyone.

I told Riley to take the rest of the week off with pay and sent her an extra two hundred and fifty dollars as a bonus for handling such a frightening situation responsibly while caring for my kids. I also told her I would cover the cost of replacing her EpiPen and any hospital bills related to the incident.

The issue is that I had originally set that money aside for my oldest child. He had just started a youth football program, and that fund was meant to cover his uniform, cleats, and other related costs. Since the money was gone, I reached out to my ex and asked if he could cover those expenses instead. He refused, saying that this was exactly what his child support payments were meant for.

I explained that the money had gone toward a medical emergency that happened while his children were in my care, and that the emergency was caused by him packing food containing nuts after being told about the allergy. He became angry and accused me of misusing child support funds, even threatening legal action. I told him he was free to pursue that if he wanted, but in the meantime, he could either help pay for the football expenses or accept that our son wouldn’t be able to participate this season.

Now he’s telling anyone who will listen that I’m irresponsible and a bad mother for giving money to a babysitter instead of spending it on our child. I’m left wondering whether I truly handled this wrong, or whether I simply responded the best I could to an emergency situation that put both my babysitter and my kids at risk.
