Was I wrong for not paying for groceries when I thought I was just helping cook?

I’m 23 and was invited by my best friend—let’s call her Lila, she’s 25—to spend a fall holiday with her family at their home. We’ve known each other for about ten years. She works a couple of low-paying jobs to save for grad school, and I’m in grad school myself with no income and a pile of student loans. Neither of us has much extra money.

I’ve spent this holiday with her family before. Every time, I help shop, prep, or cook because things get hectic, and her parents have always covered the groceries. Last year, Lila and I made a charcuterie board and a salad while her parents handled the main dishes. They paid for everything, thanked us for the help, and even complimented what we made. On other visits, I’ve run errands or helped make meals and never paid for groceries—they’ve always handled the cost.

This year, we repeated the same plan: Lila and I would tackle the charcuterie board and salad. After the holiday, though, her mom told Lila she had assumed I was going to pay for those ingredients and was upset that I didn’t. When Lila told me, I was surprised—this had never been expected before. She said not to worry and that in the future we’d just shop for the things her parents were cooking.

But the next morning, while Lila was still asleep, her mom cornered me in the living room and started scolding me about not paying. She kept repeating that I was an adult now and should contribute to what she called a “potluck.” I had no idea it was meant to be one—no one ever mentioned that. I’d noticed some relatives brought dishes, but I assumed those were like host gifts. I had already brought a pretty pricey box of chocolates as my own gift.

I apologized and even offered to repay her for the groceries, but she refused the money while still lecturing me. Later, she brought it up again in front of Lila, which made everything even more uncomfortable. If she had told Lila ahead of time that we were expected to pay this year, I would’ve planned for it—and probably wouldn’t have bought the expensive chocolates.

Now I’m feeling awkward about ever visiting again. In my culture, calling out a guest like that—especially privately while her daughter was asleep—is considered incredibly rude. If she had a concern, she could have addressed it with Lila, since she’s the one who used the card and followed their usual routine.

I’m still stunned that I was expected to know about an unspoken rule that had never applied before.
