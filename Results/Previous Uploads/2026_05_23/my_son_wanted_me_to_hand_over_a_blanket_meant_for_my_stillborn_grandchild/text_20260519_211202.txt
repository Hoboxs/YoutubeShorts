My Son Wanted Me to Hand Over a Blanket Meant for My Stillborn Grandchild.

About eight years ago, I made handmade quilts for each of my grandchildren when they were born. They were large enough for a twin bed, and every one of them took me weeks to finish. Sewing was always my thing, and I loved putting time into something personal for each child. Most of the grandkids still sleep with theirs or keep them on their beds.

The last quilt I completed was around six years ago, and even then it was difficult because my arthritis had started getting bad in my hands. Since then, sewing for long periods has become painful enough that I mostly stopped.

My son got remarried earlier this year. His wife has a ten-year-old daughter named Chloe. She’s a kind kid, and I’ve always been polite and welcoming toward her.

A few weeks ago, my son asked if I could make Chloe one of the same quilts I made for the other grandchildren. I explained that physically I just can’t do it anymore. My hands cramp badly, and even basic sewing projects are difficult now.

He then asked if I could give Chloe an unused quilt I still have stored away.

The quilt he meant was one I made years ago for my daughter’s baby. My daughter lost the baby late in the pregnancy, and the blanket was never used. I kept it packed away because emotionally I never felt right giving it away. To me, it was made for that child specifically.

I told him no. I said it would feel deeply wrong to hand that quilt to someone else, especially after what happened.

I thought that would be the end of it.

Instead, my daughter-in-law called me furious. She accused me of refusing to accept Chloe as a “real” grandchild and said I should be making more of an effort. She repeated that since I would not sew a new quilt, I should at least give Chloe the unused one sitting in storage.

I explained again that the issue was not Chloe. I physically cannot make another quilt, and the old one carries painful memories connected to my daughter’s loss.

She told me I was being unfair and treating Chloe differently from the other grandchildren. Then my son called later and said if I would not “do this one thing,” I should not expect to be involved with the kids very much moving forward.

Now I’m upset too. I understand why Chloe may feel left out seeing the other grandchildren with handmade quilts, but I also feel like people are ignoring both my physical limitations and the emotional significance of that particular blanket.

Now I’m wondering if I’m being unreasonable for refusing to give it away.
