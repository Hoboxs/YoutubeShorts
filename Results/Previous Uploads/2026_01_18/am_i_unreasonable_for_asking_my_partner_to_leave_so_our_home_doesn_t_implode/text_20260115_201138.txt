Am I Unreasonable for Asking My Partner to Leave So Our Home Doesn’t Implode?

I live with my partner and our two kids, an elementary-aged child and a toddler, and lately every evening feels like an endurance test I’m failing in slow motion. I work full time with brutally early mornings, while my partner is enrolled in an intense academic program that consumes most of her time and energy. The moment my workday ends, I become the default parent on duty: rushing to pick up the kids, juggling dinner, homework, tantrums, baths, pajamas, and bedtime, all while she retreats upstairs to study behind a closed door. On paper it sounds manageable, but the reality is pure chaos because of our youngest. The second our toddler realizes her other parent is home but inaccessible, she completely unravels. She screams for her, cries until she’s hoarse, invents excuses like suddenly needing the bathroom upstairs or a toy that “only exists” in the bedroom, and once she gets near that door, she pounds on it like the world is ending. I’m constantly dragging her away, trying to soothe her, trying to stay calm, and failing more often than I want to admit. What breaks me is that when my partner isn’t home at all, the toddler accepts it. She still misses her, but she copes. The screaming stops. The evenings are hard but survivable. When my partner is physically present yet emotionally unavailable, everything explodes. The stress builds until I feel like I’m vibrating with it. What makes this feel even more unfair is that my partner has options. She could study nearby with family, or stay on campus, or anywhere else where the kids wouldn’t be tortured by the knowledge that she’s just upstairs refusing to open the door. But she insists on studying at home every night, leaving me to manage the emotional fallout alone. Eventually, exhausted and fraying, I told her that if she plans to shut herself away for hours, it would honestly be better for everyone if she studied somewhere else, because I cannot physically or psychologically keep a toddler from wanting her parent. She was hurt and angry, said I was being unsupportive, and now I’m sitting here wracked with guilt, resentment, and bone-deep exhaustion, wondering if I’m truly asking for too much or if I’ve just become the person expected to absorb all the stress so everyone else can function.
