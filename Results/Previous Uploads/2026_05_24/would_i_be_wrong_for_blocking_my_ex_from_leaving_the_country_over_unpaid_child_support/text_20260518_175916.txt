Would I Be Wrong for Blocking My Ex From Leaving the Country Over Unpaid Child Support?

My former partner, “Daniel,” and I separated about seven years ago after a relationship that involved a lot of financial manipulation and controlling behavior.

During our marriage, I was the sole income earner while also handling nearly all of the parenting responsibilities. Daniel’s main contribution was school and daycare drop-offs. Beyond that, I managed everything else: bills, budgeting, grocery orders, meal planning, appointments, school communication, and the general mental load of running a household. I even hired a cleaner because there simply weren’t enough hours in the day.

After the divorce, Daniel eventually found work and was earning roughly what I made.

Over the past seven years, though, his involvement with the children has steadily declined. On average he’s only spent about eight percent of the time with them. At one point he saw them just thirteen times across a two-year period, entirely by his own choice.

Despite that, I worked hard to preserve the children’s relationship with his side of the family. His relatives all live overseas, and I still regularly take the children there for several weeks each year so they can stay connected to their grandparents, language, and cultural traditions. I actually visit his home country more often than he does because I built friendships and connections there during the years we lived abroad together.

Now the children live with me full time.

Daniel recently stopped working altogether, has stopped paying child support, and is currently trying to pursue shared custody through the courts. At this point he owes around six months of support payments.

Technically I can survive without the money, but things are tight. I’m budgeting carefully, and upcoming private school fees will likely have to come out of my mortgage line of credit if this continues.

Legally, I have the option to request a travel restriction order because of the unpaid support debt. If approved, he would not be allowed to leave the country until the debt is paid. Realistically, either he or his very wealthy parents would likely settle the amount immediately.

The complication is that his entire family lives overseas, including elderly grandparents who are close to one hundred years old. If one of them passed away while the restriction was active, he could miss the funeral and important cultural mourning traditions connected to it.

For what it’s worth, I would still take the children overseas for any funeral or family emergency, and I already keep emergency travel funds available for that exact reason.

Part of me feels justified because he has ignored both his financial responsibilities and much of his parental role for years. Another part of me feels guilty because I know this could seriously affect his ability to say goodbye to family members near the end of their lives.

So now I’m torn over whether enforcing the order would simply be holding him accountable, or whether it would cross a line morally.
