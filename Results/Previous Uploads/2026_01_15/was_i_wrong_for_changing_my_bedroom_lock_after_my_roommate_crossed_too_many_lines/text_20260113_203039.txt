Was I wrong for changing my bedroom lock after my roommate crossed too many lines?

I moved into a shared apartment in a city after a coworker, Lina, offered me her spare room when I was struggling to find housing. We weren’t close, but she seemed kind at first, and I was relieved to have somewhere affordable. That gratitude made me overlook a lot early on. Lina would wander into my room without asking, sit on my bed after walking around barefoot, snack on my food, and use my things like they were communal. When I tried to set boundaries casually, she laughed it off and kept doing it.

Over time, it stopped feeling harmless. I started locking my door when I left, but I kept getting this unsettling sense that someone had still been inside. When I asked her directly if she had a spare key, she snapped, accused me of being paranoid, and shut the conversation down. That was the moment I realized I didn’t feel safe in my own room anymore.

While she was out one evening, I asked a friend to replace the lock on my bedroom door. A few days later, she exploded—flooding my phone with messages about how I’d “disrespected” her, claiming I needed her permission, and saying building staff were questioning her because of me. When I got home, she yelled that she regretted letting me move in and said I’d be forced out.

I went to the building office myself the next day. They told me she already had multiple complaints from past roommates involving intimidation and missing property. They also confirmed I had every right to change the lock on my private room. After that, I filed a formal report. A notice was issued to her almost immediately, and while the apartment is tense now, I finally feel secure.

I didn’t want conflict—I wanted boundaries. I just didn’t expect protecting myself to turn into a full-blown war.
