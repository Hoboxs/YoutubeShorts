Was I wrong for not stepping in when my child was deliberately left out?

I’m a parent in my late thirties with two daughters, one older and neurotypical and one younger who is autistic. This situation involves my younger child. She struggles a lot socially and, despite ongoing therapy, she can be blunt, disruptive, and unintentionally cruel because she doesn’t understand social cues well. At her school, there’s a strict rule: if birthday invitations are handed out in class and the whole class is invited, no one can be excluded. Recently, a boy in her class—someone she has a long history of conflict with—was handing out invitations at school. My daughter came home in tears, convinced she was the only child not invited. Her special education teacher contacted me and said the school could intervene since the rule was violated, but I chose not to escalate it. I explained to my daughter that she and this boy don’t get along, asked why she even wanted to attend his party, and pointed out how upset she would be if she were forced to invite someone she disliked. She had a meltdown and said it was unfair, but my partner and I agreed that forcing another child to include her wasn’t right, even if it hurt.

Later, a virtual meeting with the teacher and the boy’s parent revealed far more. The boy’s parent was confused, explaining that the party was small and only for close friends, not the entire class. When questioned, the boy admitted he had deliberately made it look like everyone was invited and lied to my daughter, telling her she alone was excluded. He said he did it to hurt her because of a serious bullying incident before winter break, one that he never reported because he felt nothing ever happened when my daughter was involved. Hearing this was devastating. I apologized immediately, asked the school to formally document and address my child’s behavior, and requested that they not explain the lie to her so I could handle it myself. Now I’m conflicted—part of me believes shielding her from consequences hasn’t helped her learn, and part of me wonders if allowing her to sit with this pain makes me a bad parent instead of a realistic one.
