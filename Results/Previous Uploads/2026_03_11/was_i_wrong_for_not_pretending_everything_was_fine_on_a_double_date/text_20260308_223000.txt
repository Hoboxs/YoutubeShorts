Was I wrong for not pretending everything was fine on a double date?

Years ago I worked with a woman I’ll call Erin. She used to tell me all the time that I’d be perfect for her friend Sabrina. It came up so often that it almost felt like a running joke between us. Every time she mentioned it, the conversation would go about the same way.

At first I’d ask basic questions like, “Is she cute?” Over time I got a little more thoughtful and started asking things like what we had in common. Erin would usually roll her eyes and say things like, “You’re both tall,” or that we shared a similar sense of humor. Erin herself was very attractive and tended to hang around with equally attractive friends. One of them, Sabrina, was a tall blonde who definitely looked out of my league. I kept hoping Erin would actually set us up, but while we worked together it never happened.

A few years later I ran into Erin again. She was working at a boutique wine shop near my office, and I’d see her from time to time. Eventually she brought up Sabrina again and repeated how well we’d get along. This time I pushed a little harder and said I’d genuinely be interested in meeting her.

Erin repeated some of the same details: that Sabrina was tall, had a similar sense of humor, and worked at a nearby restaurant. She even mentioned that one time at work I had talked about a TV show, and later that evening Sabrina had apparently said the exact same things about it when they were hanging out. That made it seem like we really might click.

We exchanged numbers and later that day she texted me with a plan: dinner at a restaurant with her, her fiancé, Sabrina, and me. I looked at Erin’s social media and it seemed pretty clear which Sabrina she meant, the same tall, very conventionally attractive friend I’d always heard about.

The morning of the date Erin messaged saying we had to postpone. No big deal, things happen. We rescheduled for the following week.

When the night finally came, I really tried to make a good impression. I got a haircut, picked out a nice outfit, and even looked over the restaurant menu ahead of time so I wouldn’t seem awkward.

When I arrived, though, the woman introduced as Sabrina was clearly not the same person Erin had been describing. She was much shorter, heavier, and worked at a daycare rather than a restaurant. It was obvious from everyone’s reactions that my surprise and disappointment showed on my face.

Instead of confronting anyone directly, I just kept referring back to the things I had been told before. I asked questions like when she stopped watching that TV show we supposedly both loved, how long she had been waitressing, and how long she had known Erin since Erin had mentioned they were childhood friends.

Erin kept trying to steer the conversation away from those topics, but Sabrina seemed increasingly uncomfortable.

At one point the two women went to the restroom, and Erin’s fiancé pulled me aside and asked what I was doing. I told him it seemed pretty obvious Erin had meant to set me up with a different person originally.

He admitted that was basically true, the original Sabrina had kept postponing and clearly wasn’t interested, so Erin decided to set me up with someone else instead. He even joked that she had found another woman with the same name who was “more in my league.”

The whole thing left a bad taste in my mouth, and apparently the woman on the date felt embarrassed too.

Some friends later told me I should have just played along politely instead of making it obvious that something was off.

Was I wrong for reacting the way I did instead of pretending nothing had changed?
