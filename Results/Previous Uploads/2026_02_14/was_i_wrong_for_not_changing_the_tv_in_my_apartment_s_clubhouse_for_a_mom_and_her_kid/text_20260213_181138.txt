Was I wrong for not changing the TV in my apartment’s clubhouse for a mom and her kid?

I’m 25 and live in an apartment complex that has a shared clubhouse with a big TV. I work from home, so I use the space pretty often. At some point, I logged into one of my streaming accounts on the clubhouse TV and even made a separate profile specifically labeled for residents so other people could use it if they wanted.

On Super Bowl Sunday, I walked over to the clubhouse expecting it to be packed. Surprisingly, it was completely empty. I’m not even a huge football fan, but since it’s such a big event, I figured someone would show up eventually to watch. So I turned the game on and sat down, planning to watch for a bit and then leave it on for whoever came by later.

About halfway through the second quarter, a woman in her 40s came in with her young son — maybe three years old. She sat right next to me. I assumed she was there for the game. Instead, she sighed and asked, “Are you watching this?”

Before I could fully answer, she started looking for the remote and said, “We don’t have a smart TV at home, so we’d like to use YouTube on this TV.”

Now here’s the thing: I truly did not care about the Super Bowl. I had zero emotional investment. I could have walked back to my apartment and been perfectly fine. But something about her tone immediately rubbed me the wrong way. It felt less like a request and more like an assumption that I’d move.

So I said, “Yes, I’m watching this. It’s the Super Bowl tonight.”

She replied, “Right… but we can’t get what we need on TV at home, and the Super Bowl is on everywhere. So do you mind?”

I told her I understood, but I had gotten there first and was enjoying my time. That’s when she said this was a “COMMUNITY” space and that we needed to respect each other in communal areas, and that it wasn’t respectful to “hoard” it.

I responded that if she planned to use a communal space for something specific on a major TV night, she should’ve made sure she arrived first. I even invited her to stay and watch, mentioning the halftime show was coming up.

She ended up moving to another table and sat on her phone while her son kept asking to watch his show. I’ll admit, hearing him ask made me feel guilty. After about 30 minutes of tense silence, she muttered something under her breath and left.

Looking back, I know I could have just given in. I didn’t actually care about the game. I think my reaction was more about feeling pushed than about football. But at the same time, it is a shared space, and I was already there using it for a pretty widely expected event.

So now I’m wondering if I let pride win over practicality. Was I wrong for standing my ground when I could have easily gone home and let the kid watch YouTube?
