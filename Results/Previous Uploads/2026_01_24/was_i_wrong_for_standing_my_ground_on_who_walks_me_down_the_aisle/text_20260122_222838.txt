Was I wrong for standing my ground on who walks me down the aisle?

I grew up in a crowded household in a small coastal town in another country, the middle child of four kids. My parents split when I was about eight, and my father vanished from our daily lives not long after. I was left with fragments of memories and a lot of anger that hardened as I got older. My older sister and younger brother barely spoke his name, and my youngest sibling treated him like a villain from a story we all agreed to hate. My mother encouraged that anger. She carried it loudly and often, and while she provided food and shelter, her words were sharp and unforgiving. Over time, the constant criticism and the lies she told to protect her version of events wore away any trust I had in her.

When I turned nineteen, my father reached out and asked if I would meet him for tea. I almost said no. Years of resentment do not disappear just because someone asks nicely. Still, curiosity and a quiet hope pushed me to agree. He apologized without excuses and told me he wanted to try again, even if that meant moving slowly. I did not forgive him right away, but I chose to listen. Over time, his actions stayed consistent. He checked in, showed up when he said he would, and supported me through school, job changes, and heartbreaks. When my siblings reached adulthood, he contacted them too. They refused, and I respected that decision because everyone heals differently.

As I grew closer to my father, my relationship with my mother continued to fracture. She disapproved of almost every choice I made, especially my partner. She is deeply religious, and my fiancé is not, which in her eyes makes him unacceptable. Family dinners became tense, phone calls turned into lectures, and I found myself bracing for criticism whenever her name appeared on my screen.

Now, at twenty nine, I am planning my wedding. It should be joyful, but instead it has become a battlefield. I invited my father and asked him to walk me down the aisle. To me, that moment represents the relationship we rebuilt and the support he has given me when I needed it most. When my family found out, everything exploded. My siblings accused me of betraying our childhood pain. My mother demanded that he be removed entirely and insisted that she should take his place instead.

They gave me an ultimatum. If my father attends, they will not. They said I was being cruel, selfish, and disrespectful to the past. I listened, I argued back, and eventually I reached my limit. I told them that my wedding is not a stage for old grudges and that I will not erase someone who has been there for me just to keep the peace. If they choose not to come, that is their decision.

Now I am left with the weight of that choice. I feel calm about honoring my own boundaries, but the silence from my family is heavy. I cannot help wondering if standing firm makes me heartless, or if choosing my happiness is something I was always allowed to do.
