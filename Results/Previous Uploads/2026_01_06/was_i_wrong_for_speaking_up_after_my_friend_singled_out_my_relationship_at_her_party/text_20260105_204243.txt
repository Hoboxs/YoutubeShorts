Was I Wrong for Speaking Up After My Friend Singled Out My Relationship at Her Party?

I’m 23 and have been living in the same city for a few years now. I’ve been with my partner, Liam (25), for just over two years. We live together, share a routine, and usually attend social events as a unit—not because we’re glued to each other, but because our lives are genuinely intertwined. My friend “Hannah” (23) and I have known each other since our late teens, back when we were classmates and bonded over complaining about school and dreaming about independence. We’ve had minor disagreements before, but nothing that ever hinted at what happened here.

Hannah threw herself a big birthday party at a rented venue in the city. It was clearly planned to feel important: printed seating charts, assigned tables, decorations everywhere. Around thirty-five people were invited. Weeks earlier, I RSVP’d for both me and Liam. When we arrived and checked the seating list, I felt my stomach drop. I was assigned to Hannah’s table with her closest friends, front and center. Liam, meanwhile, was seated on the opposite side of the room with people he didn’t know at all. At first, I genuinely assumed it was a mistake.

I pulled Hannah aside quietly, expecting her to apologize and fix it. Instead, she told me it was intentional. She said she didn’t want couples “stuck together” and wanted people to “socialize more.” That explanation collapsed instantly when I looked around and realized every other couple—some who had been dating only a few months—was seated together. When I pointed this out, she finally admitted the truth: she thought Liam would “drag the energy down” and said I’d be “more fun” without him around. For context, Liam is quiet but kind, polite, and has never caused tension at any gathering.

Once the dinner started, the awkwardness became impossible to ignore. Liam sat stiffly at his table, smiling too hard, clearly aware that he was the only person separated from their partner. He tried to joke about it when he caught my eye, but I could tell he was embarrassed and uncomfortable. It felt less like a seating choice and more like a public statement about how unwelcome he was.

I confronted Hannah again, still privately, and told her that deliberately singling us out—especially while seating everyone else with their partners—was hurtful and unnecessary. I also said her comments about Liam were unfair. She rolled her eyes, told me it was her birthday, and said I was being dramatic and selfish.

I didn’t cause a scene. We stayed. We ate, smiled, and clapped at the right moments. But I was quieter than usual, and the night felt heavy instead of celebratory. Since then, Hannah has been flooding my phone with messages accusing me of “ruining her birthday,” embarrassing her, and making everything about my relationship. Some mutual friends agree with her and say I should’ve kept my feelings to myself for the sake of the party. Others think what she did was intentional and cruel, and that I was right to say something.

I didn’t want to create drama—but I also didn’t want to silently accept my partner being publicly excluded and judged. I’m still torn, and I keep replaying the night in my head, wondering if I crossed a line or if I simply refused to stay quiet when I should have.
