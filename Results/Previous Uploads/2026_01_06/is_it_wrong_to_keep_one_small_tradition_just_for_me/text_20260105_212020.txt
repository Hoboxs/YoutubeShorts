Is It Wrong to Keep One Small Tradition Just for Me?

I share parenting duties with my partner, and we alternate mornings getting our child ready for school, including packing lunches. On the days it’s my turn, I sometimes slip a small handwritten note into the lunch bag. They’re nothing elaborate—just quiet little messages about a book we’re reading together, a plan for the weekend, or a simple reminder that I’m proud and thinking about them. It’s become a soft, private ritual that feels very mine, something tender I do without announcement or expectation.

One morning, my partner happened to walk in while I was writing one of these notes. He paused, watched for a second, and then asked if I could start signing them from both of us instead of just me. I told him, gently, that I’d rather not. I explained that this wasn’t meant to be a shared statement or a family announcement—it was just a personal message. I added that if he wanted to write his own notes on his days, I thought that would be lovely. I even slid a scrap of paper across the counter to make the point that I wasn’t stopping him from doing anything similar.

That’s when things shifted. He stiffened, said he shouldn’t have to write his own note, and accused me of shutting him out. He compared it to excluding him from signing holiday cards or claiming gifts as mine alone. His voice rose, frustration spilling out as he insisted it was the same thing. To me, it didn’t feel the same at all—this wasn’t a public gesture or a shared milestone, just a quiet moment of connection.

The argument lingered in the air long after breakfast was over. I was left wondering whether protecting this tiny, personal tradition made me selfish, or whether it’s okay for each parent to have their own ways of showing love instead of merging everything into a single signature.
