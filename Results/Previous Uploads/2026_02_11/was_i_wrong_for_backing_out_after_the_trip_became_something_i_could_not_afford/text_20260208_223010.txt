Was I wrong for backing out after the trip became something I could not afford?

A few months after a really fun group getaway earlier this year, three of us started talking about planning another trip for the following year. By early autumn, we were excited and began outlining a simple, budget friendly weekend plan that worked for all of us financially and schedule wise. We were all on the same page and looking forward to it.

Later on, we realized the specific activity we had planned would work better with four people instead of three. One friend suggested inviting someone she knew, and the rest of us agreed. At first, everything continued smoothly, and the new person seemed easygoing and enthusiastic about the original plan.

Then, about a month later, she proposed changing the destination entirely. The new location was farther away, more expensive, and would turn a short weekend into a much longer trip. One friend immediately supported the change, while another friend and I were hesitant. For a while, the group was split evenly, but within a few days, I became the only person still uncomfortable with the new plan.

I explained clearly and more than once that the updated destination would nearly double what I could afford to spend. I also shared that extending the trip into an extra week simply was not possible for me financially or with my responsibilities. I said that if the group really wanted to go ahead with the new plan, I understood, but I would need to step back. I was told that opting out would ruin the idea of traveling as a group of four.

To be clear, the new destination did sound great. If money and time were not an issue, I would have loved to go. But I was honest about my limits and did not want to stretch myself beyond what I could reasonably manage. I even spoke to each of the original friends privately, hoping we could compromise or revisit the original plan, but by then, the decision had already been made without me.

After weeks of feeling unheard, I finally said I would cancel my participation because I could not afford the trip and did not want to put myself in financial stress. This was about two weeks before the planned date. With such short notice, finding affordable accommodations for the remaining group became difficult. Eventually, they decided not to go at all, saying there was no point in settling for something subpar when the trip was no longer what they had planned.

Now, it feels like the blame has quietly landed on me. The trip did not happen, and I cannot shake the feeling that everyone sees me as the reason it fell apart. At the same time, I know I was honest from the beginning about my budget and tried to communicate clearly and respectfully.

I feel guilty, but I also feel like I was put in a position where my only options were to overspend or walk away. I am still unsure whether standing my ground was reasonable or if I truly spoiled something for everyone else.
