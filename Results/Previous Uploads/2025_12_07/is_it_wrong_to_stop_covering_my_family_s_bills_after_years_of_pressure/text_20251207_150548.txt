Is It Wrong to Stop Covering My Family’s Bills After Years of Pressure?

I’m a 29-year-old Black woman working as a technical assistant. I earn enough to live steadily on my own, but I’m far from wealthy. Ever since I finished school and got my first real paycheck, my relatives have expected me to step in financially—paying for household expenses, helping nieces and nephews with supplies, sending money to my father every month, and handling sudden expenses whenever they popped up.

In my community, this kind of ongoing support—often referred to as “Black tax”—is considered normal and even honorable. I understood that growing up, and for a long time I didn’t question it. But over the last year, everything intensified. Suddenly I was responsible for covering utility bills, taking care of my cousin’s medical copay, and even contributing toward a family friend’s engagement ceremony.

Meanwhile, I’ve been putting my own life on hold. I want to save for my own home, invest in my career, and afford counseling. I told my family that I would still help with true necessities, but I couldn’t keep financing everyone’s lifestyle. My father insisted I was turning my back on my “duties.” An aunt accused me of trying to act “too independent.” My younger cousin said I was selfish because I “make the most money.”

But the truth is, they’ve been able to splurge on new clothes and weekend trips while I was draining my savings to bail them out. I love them deeply, yet I’m exhausted and feel like I’ve been expected to sacrifice my entire adulthood for everyone else’s comfort.

Now I’m questioning whether finally choosing to prioritize my own stability makes me wrong—or whether my family is simply upset that I’m no longer willing to carry the entire load alone.
