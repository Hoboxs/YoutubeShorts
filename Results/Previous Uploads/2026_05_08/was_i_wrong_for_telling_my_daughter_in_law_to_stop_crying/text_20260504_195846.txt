Was I wrong for telling my daughter in law to stop crying?

I am being pressured to apologize to my daughter in law, but I honestly do not feel like I did anything terrible, and now I am wondering if I am missing something. She is an extremely emotional person, and to put it plainly, almost any kind of criticism or correction ends with her in tears. It has become difficult for the whole family because even the smallest issue somehow turns into a situation where everyone rushes to comfort her while the other person gets treated like a villain.

There have been many moments over the years that built up this frustration for me. One example that still stands out happened at my house when she walked through with muddy shoes on. I calmly asked her to take them off, and instead of simply doing it, she burst into tears because she said my tone upset her. I did not yell, insult her, or embarrass her. My daughter was there and even agreed I had spoken normally. Yet somehow it became an emotional ordeal, and afterward my son lectured me about being more careful with her feelings.

That pattern has repeated itself over and over. The crying is never subtle either. It is loud, immediate, and impossible to ignore, which instantly changes the atmosphere in the room. It feels like everyone has to stop what they are doing to comfort her while whoever upset her gets blamed, even when the original issue was minor.

The situation that caused this latest conflict happened during a family birthday party for my granddaughter. There were children everywhere, and one little boy there has a reputation for saying rude things without thinking. His parents are actively working on it, but he is still very young and has no filter.

At one point during the party, my daughter in law was helping hand out food when this child bluntly called her fat. His parents reacted immediately and pulled him aside, clearly embarrassed. But my daughter in law started crying right there in the middle of the party, and it quickly became loud enough that people were staring.

I was worried it was going to completely derail the birthday celebration, so I took her aside privately and told her she needed to calm down. I said it was a comment from a very young child and that she should not let it turn into a scene at a little girl’s birthday party. I also told her not to come back out until she had composed herself.

Instead, she went to sit in the car and never returned to the party. Later, my son confronted me angrily, saying I had been insensitive and cruel. From my perspective, though, she is an adult, and the comment came from a child barely old enough to understand what he was saying.

Now my son insists I owe her an apology, but I still feel like I was trying to keep the focus on the birthday instead of letting the entire event become centered around her emotions. At the same time, I know I can come across as blunt, and I am starting to wonder if I handled the situation more harshly than I intended.
