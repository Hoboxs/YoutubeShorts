Am I wrong for asking my Spanish teacher to stop using the Spanish version of my nickname?

My legal name is Jonathan Paul, but I’ve gone by JP for as long as I can remember. That’s what my friends call me, what my coaches used in middle school, and what every teacher from freshman year onward has written on attendance sheets. It’s the name that feels natural to me.

This year, though, my Spanish teacher has decided to call me something different. In class, instead of saying JP, he pronounces each letter the Spanish way, “Jota Pe.” The first time he said it, everyone laughed, including me. I assumed it was just a quick joke to illustrate pronunciation differences between English and Spanish.

But it didn’t stop there. Every single day during roll call, during group activities, when asking me questions, it’s always “Jota Pe.” At first I tried to ignore it, but after a few weeks it started to grate on me. It doesn’t feel like my name anymore; it feels like a classroom bit that never ends.

I spoke to him after class and politely asked if he could just call me JP, since that’s what I prefer. He told me that because this is Spanish class, he uses the Spanish pronunciation of names when possible, and that “Jota Pe” is simply the Spanish equivalent of my initials. He emphasized that it’s not an insult, just translation.

I understand the logic, and I know he’s not trying to mock me. Still, it’s my name. I don’t feel comfortable being called something I didn’t choose, even if it’s technically accurate in another language. I live in the U.S., and none of my other teachers translate my nickname into different pronunciations.

Now I’m wondering if I’m being overly sensitive about something harmless, or if it’s reasonable to expect a teacher to respect the name I prefer to go by.

So, am I wrong for wanting him to stop?
