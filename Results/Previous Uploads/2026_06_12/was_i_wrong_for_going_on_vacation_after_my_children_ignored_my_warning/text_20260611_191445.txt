Was I Wrong for Going on Vacation After My Children Ignored My Warning?

My wife and I became parents very young, and for most of our adult lives we focused almost entirely on raising our family. While I worked long hours, my wife stayed home and devoted herself to our children. By the time our youngest finished university, we both felt like we had finally reached a stage of life where we could slow down, travel, and enjoy some freedom together.

That didn’t last very long.

Within a few years, all four of our children had started families of their own. We were thrilled to become grandparents, and at first we were happy to help whenever we could. When our oldest daughter asked us to watch her son occasionally, it seemed reasonable. Then our other children started asking too.

What began as occasional babysitting gradually evolved into something much larger.

Before we knew it, my wife and I were caring for five grandchildren almost every weekday. We handled breakfast, school drop-offs, pickups, homework supervision, sports practices, dinners, baths, and bedtime routines. Some mornings started before dawn, and some evenings didn’t end until nearly 10:00 p.m. We were effectively functioning as full-time childcare providers while also trying to maintain our own household and personal lives.

We love our grandchildren, but the arrangement became exhausting. We felt like we had moved directly from raising our own children into raising another generation without ever getting a chance to breathe.

Eventually, my wife told me she couldn’t continue at that pace. I agreed, and we decided to finally take a long-delayed holiday together. We booked a two-and-a-half-week trip through Spain and Portugal and informed all of our children more than a month in advance. We made it very clear that we would be unavailable and that they needed to arrange alternative childcare.

Every one of them acknowledged the plan.

The morning we were scheduled to leave, however, something unbelievable happened.

Despite all the warnings, our children still dropped their kids off at our house as if nothing had changed. Some of them left so quickly that they barely stayed long enough for a conversation. It felt like they genuinely expected us to cancel our trip and continue providing childcare simply because they hadn’t made other arrangements.

Rather than abandon the children, we took them to school as normal that morning. Before leaving, we informed the school administration that neither my wife nor I would be available for pickup and that parents needed to be contacted directly. We assumed our children would finally take the situation seriously once they realized we weren’t bluffing.

Hours later, we learned that none of them had immediately responded to the school.

By then, we were already several hours into our drive toward the airport.

Our phones started exploding with calls and messages. Some were angry, others were panicked, and a few accused us of being irresponsible. I replied to one group message reminding everyone that they had been informed multiple times about our travel plans and had weeks to arrange alternative care. After that, I turned my phone off and focused on the trip.

When we returned home, the fallout was enormous.

Several of our children accused us of abandoning the family. They argued that grandparents should be willing to step in when needed and claimed we had put the schools and the children in an unfair position. On the other hand, a few relatives pointed out that we had given extensive notice and that our children had simply ignored us because they assumed we would cave at the last minute.

I genuinely love my grandchildren and would never intentionally put them in a bad situation. At the same time, I feel like my wife and I have spent years sacrificing our own lives to make things easier for our children, and when we finally asked for a few weeks to ourselves, nobody respected that boundary.

Now I’m wondering whether we were wrong for following through with our vacation after our children ignored our warnings, or whether they were responsible for the situation they created.
