Was I wrong for not telling people that characters in my friend’s novel were based on us?

My best friend, “Maya,” is an author with a few published novels. She has a habit of weaving traits from people she loves into her characters, small things like personality quirks, physical features, or shared memories. It’s never a one-to-one copy, but the inspiration is there.

In her latest book, though, one character was very obviously inspired by me. Similar temperament, similar appearance, and even some parallel life experiences. The romantic lead opposite that character was clearly inspired by another close friend of ours, “Jordan.” In the story, those two characters end up in a relationship.

For context, the three of us met in college. Jordan and I have never dated or had any romantic involvement in real life.

I knew about the similarities because Maya and I have a tradition where I read the third draft of her manuscripts before publication. Jordan isn’t much of a reader, so I didn’t think to mention it. I also didn’t bring it up to my husband. In my mind, these were fictional characters, inspired by real people, yes, but not actually us.

The book was published, and I didn’t give it much thought.

A while later, we were all at another friend’s place, and Jordan happened to be there. Maya asked if Jordan had read the book. Jordan said yes and mentioned being surprised that “we ended up together” in the story, adding that it felt a little strange.

My husband overheard and asked what that meant. That’s when Maya casually explained that the characters were based on Jordan and me.

Both Jordan and my husband were taken aback. They asked why I hadn’t given them a heads-up. They said they felt blindsided and uncomfortable, especially since the characters’ relationship was portrayed in detail.

I explained that I don’t usually share information about Maya’s projects before release, and that the characters are fictional, not literal representations of us. Most readers wouldn’t make the connection unless they knew us personally.

Even so, they’re still uneasy about it.

Was I wrong for not warning them ahead of time?
