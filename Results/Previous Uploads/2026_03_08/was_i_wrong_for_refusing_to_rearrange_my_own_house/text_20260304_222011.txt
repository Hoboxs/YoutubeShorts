Was I wrong for refusing to rearrange my own house?

I’m posting anonymously because my housemate follows my usual account. I’m a 31-year-old woman who inherited a four-bedroom cottage in a small town in northern England after my uncle passed away three years ago. He didn’t have any children, and we were very close, so he left the property to me. I spent nearly eighteen months remodeling it, repainting, redoing the floors, and turning it into a place that truly felt like mine. Around the time I finished, a longtime friend of mine, also 31, split from her fiancé and suddenly had nowhere to live. I offered her a place to stay without hesitation. I gave her the second-largest bedroom, about 15 square meters, and it has its own ensuite shower. I kept the primary bedroom for myself, turned the smallest bedroom into a studio since I work remotely as a graphic designer, and converted the attic into a hobby space where I host a weekly board game night with friends every Friday. I told her she could decorate her room however she liked and that she was free to use the kitchen, lounge, and backyard whenever she wanted. The only areas I asked her not to enter without permission were my bedroom, my studio, and the attic room. I don’t charge her rent, she just contributes toward electricity, water, and shared groceries. I also asked for basic courtesy: a heads-up about overnight guests, keeping noise down after 10 p.m. on weeknights, and maintaining her own room. After about fourteen months of living together with no obvious issues, she suddenly told me she feels cramped and that it’s unfair she only has her bedroom and bathroom plus shared spaces, while I have multiple private rooms in addition to the communal areas. I was taken aback because she’d never expressed dissatisfaction before. I reminded her gently that the house is mine and that the layout was established before she moved in. She became upset and said we need to “reconsider” how the rooms are divided. Now I feel uneasy because I stepped in to help her when she was struggling, and while I’m happy she feels comfortable here, I can’t shake the feeling that asking me to redistribute space in my own home crosses a line.
