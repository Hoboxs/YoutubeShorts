Am I wrong for giving my niece more than her step siblings?

I have found myself in the middle of a family situation that is becoming increasingly uncomfortable, and I genuinely do not know whether I am being unfair or simply maintaining reasonable boundaries. I am forty years old, and years ago I joined a small technology company very early on that eventually became incredibly successful. When it was sold, it changed my family’s financial future permanently. I am not endlessly wealthy, but I live very comfortably, and I have always wanted to share that good fortune with the people closest to me.

I have an older brother, and he has a daughter named Lily who is now in her early teens. I have always been especially close to her. I only have boys, and they absolutely adore their cousin. When she was younger, she spent a lot of time around our house helping out with the younger children and becoming a huge part of our daily lives. Because of that closeness, my husband and I naturally started doing things for her over the years.

Even before our financial situation improved dramatically, we helped pay for her education and included her in family vacations. As our lifestyle changed, so did the opportunities we could offer her. We created a large education fund for her future, made sure she would be able to pursue advanced degrees if she wanted, and planned for her to eventually have financial support toward buying a home one day. We also travel often and frequently bring her along on international trips and holidays. Her parents have always been supportive and grateful, and until recently, there had never been tension about it.

The issue started after Lily’s mother remarried a few years ago. Her husband has two children from a previous relationship, both teenagers around Lily’s age. Their household struggles financially, and while everyone gets along reasonably well on the surface, the differences between the children’s lifestyles have become impossible to ignore.

Lily attends a private school, travels internationally, and experiences things the other children simply do not. Meanwhile, her step siblings attend public school and live a much more modest life. Apparently resentment has been building for years, especially from the stepfather, who feels it is unfair that one child in the household receives opportunities far beyond what the others can access.

Recently, Lily’s mother approached me very carefully and admitted that her husband wanted her to speak to me. She asked whether my husband and I would consider paying for the other children’s schooling as well or including them more equally in gifts and vacations so the differences would not feel so extreme within the home.

She made it very clear she personally understood that these children are not my responsibility, but she also said her husband believes it is cold and unfair to treat children living under the same roof so differently.

The problem is that I barely know these children. They are not family members I helped raise or built years of closeness with. I do not dislike them at all, but my relationship with them is nowhere near what I have with my niece. From my perspective, the support we give Lily comes from a deeply personal bond, not some obligation to provide equally for every child connected to her household.

Still, I cannot ignore the emotional reality of the situation. I understand how difficult it must be for those children to constantly compare their lives to hers. At the same time, I feel uncomfortable with the idea that my generosity toward one child suddenly creates an expectation that I should financially support others I hardly know.

Now I am questioning whether I am being reasonable for drawing that line, or if continuing this imbalance inside their blended family makes me more responsible for the tension than I want to admit.
