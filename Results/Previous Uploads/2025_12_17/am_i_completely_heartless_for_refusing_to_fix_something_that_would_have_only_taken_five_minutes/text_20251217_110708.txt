Am I completely heartless for refusing to fix something that “would’ve only taken five minutes”?

I work as a licensed electrician and I’m part of a union, something I’ve spent years building my career around. My former spouse and I share custody of our three children, and while things are civil now, our split last year was anything but easy. Not long after the divorce was finalized, she remarried a man she’d been seeing for less than a year. I’ll call him Derek.

The tension really started bubbling up a few weeks ago at my daughter’s volleyball game. Derek decided it was the perfect setting to loudly share his opinions about how “real tradesmen don’t need unions” and how union workers are just overpaid and lazy. He said this while standing a few feet from me, fully aware that I was wearing a shirt with my union’s logo across the chest. I didn’t respond. I just swallowed it for the sake of my kid. Derek works in a loosely related field, doing small freelance tech and networking jobs, but he’s not licensed at the level I am and struggles to keep steady income. Ironically, my certification allows me to legally perform every kind of job he does—and at a significantly higher professional standard.

Then, out of nowhere, my ex called me early one morning. She explained that there was an issue with an electrical panel at one of Derek’s job sites and asked if I could “just swing by” and take a look. She framed it as a tiny favor, something that would only take a few minutes, insisting it wasn’t a real job and that I’d technically be helping Derek out. She emphasized how easy it would be, how little effort it would take, and how much it would mean to them.

I told her calmly that I don’t do unpaid work, especially not side jobs, and that union rules require any work I do to go through my shop at a standard rate. I said Derek was welcome to hire me like any other client. That’s when her tone changed. She accused me of “making things difficult on purpose” and asked if I was really going to cause problems with her husband over something so small. I replied that Derek could hire his own subcontractor or pay me fairly like everyone else does. I also made it clear that I don’t offer free labor to someone who openly disrespects my profession.

Now I’m being told I’m petty, vindictive, and intentionally creating tension, especially because Derek lives with my kids. Apparently, setting boundaries and expecting professional respect is somehow me “starting drama.” Meanwhile, I’m left wondering when basic self-respect became something I was supposed to sacrifice just to keep the peace.
