Is It Wrong to Ban a Christmas Turkey From Our Oven?

Every Christmas, my spouse and I host the holiday at our home so our young children can stay put, open their gifts, and enjoy the day without being dragged around. We’ve done this for years, and it’s become the expected routine for her side of the family. Both of us are lifelong vegetarians—not preachy or confrontational, but deeply uncomfortable with the realities of the meat industry. We don’t stop others from eating what they choose; we just draw boundaries in our own home.

Most of her relatives are also vegetarian, but her father and one sibling aren’t. In past years, they handled this by cooking their turkey at their own house and reheating it in our microwave. That worked fine, because we never used the microwave ourselves and didn’t really care. This year, however, the microwave broke, and since we have no use for it, we didn’t replace it.

Now my father-in-law insists that the turkey either be cooked or reheated in our oven instead. I said no. I explained that we don’t want our oven—or our air fryer—used for cooking meat, especially something as pungent as turkey, because the smell alone makes us uncomfortable. I even offered a compromise: they could bring their own air fryer or make other arrangements. That suggestion was dismissed as unreasonable, and I was told we were putting “silly beliefs” ahead of the Christmas spirit.

I’ve tried to be clear that we are not banning meat from our house. They are free to eat whatever they want here. We’re simply asking that our own appliances not be used to cook it. The pushback has gotten so intense that I’m honestly considering buying a new microwave just to avoid the conflict entirely, even though it feels unfair to have to do that.

So now I’m stuck wondering whether holding this boundary makes me unreasonable—or whether it’s okay to say no, even at Christmas.
