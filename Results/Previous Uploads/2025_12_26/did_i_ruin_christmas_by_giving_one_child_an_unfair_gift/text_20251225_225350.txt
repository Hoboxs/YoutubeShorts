Did I Ruin Christmas by Giving One Child an Unfair Gift?

A few weeks before the holidays, my partner and I decided to surprise our only child with something big: a ticket to a live show just before the new year. It wasn’t a casual gift. The venue is several hours away, required an overnight stay, time off work, and careful planning. I was going too, partly because she’s still young, partly because I genuinely love the artist, and partly because it felt like one of those rare, memory-making moments parents hope for. We wrapped the confirmation nicely and gave it to her early, and her reaction was pure joy.

Not long after, my partner’s sister called to chat about holiday plans. During the conversation, I mentioned the concert, not thinking much of it. There was a long pause, then a quiet comment about how her child would have loved something like that. She asked questions—where it was, when it was, how exciting it sounded—and eventually said outright that her kid would have been over the moon to go. By then, the ticket was already given, the plans already set, and my heart already sinking.

After we hung up, the guilt hit hard. I checked the website and saw there were still tickets available. But buying another one wouldn’t have been simple. That would mean explaining why only one of the other children was included, arranging extra travel, supervision, and expenses we hadn’t budgeted for, and quietly turning a carefully planned parent-child experience into something completely different. Our family circumstances aren’t the same, financially or logistically, and this gift was something we chose intentionally for our own child.

Now Christmas dinner is approaching, and the excitement in my house feels tangled with dread. I can already picture the moment when the story comes up, the comparison, the disappointment. I didn’t mean to exclude anyone, but I can’t undo what’s already been given. What was supposed to be a joyful surprise now feels like a silent holiday landmine waiting to go off.
