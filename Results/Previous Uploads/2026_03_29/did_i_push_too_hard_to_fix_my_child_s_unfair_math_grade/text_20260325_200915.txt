Did I push too hard to fix my child’s unfair math grade?

I am a father of a ten year old girl who is in elementary school, and math has been a real struggle for her this year. She has had a particularly hard time with multiplication and division, and things only became more confusing once her class started working with larger numbers. We tried extra help through the school, but it did not seem to make much of a difference. The method they were teaching in class involved breaking numbers into parts, drawing boxes, and then combining everything at the end. For my daughter, that process just did not click. She would get most of it wrong and end up frustrated.

At home, I decided to show her a different approach, the one I had learned when I was her age. Almost immediately, something changed. She started to understand what she was doing, and her homework became far less stressful. She was finally able to solve the problems correctly and felt more confident.

Then came a test that changed everything. She came home upset, saying she had received a very low mark. When I looked at the test, I was shocked. She had only made two mistakes out of a large number of questions, yet her score was barely above half. It did not make sense at all. When I spoke with her teacher, I was told that the issue was not the answers but the method. Because my daughter had not used the specific technique taught in class, most of her work was marked wrong. I pointed out that the test only required students to show their work, not follow one exact process. The teacher stood firm and even warned that future tests could receive a zero if the method was not followed.

That response did not sit well with me. I felt like my daughter was being punished for finally understanding the material in a way that worked for her. So I went to the school administration and made it clear that I believed the grading was unfair. I will admit that I was persistent and did not let the issue drop. Eventually, a meeting was held, and the decision was made to adjust her grade to reflect her correct answers. They also confirmed that going forward, she would be allowed to solve problems in any clear way as long as she showed her work.

While this resolved the issue for my daughter, it created tension elsewhere. The teacher was clearly unhappy with the outcome, and my partner believes I made the situation bigger than it needed to be. She keeps saying that teachers have a lot on their plate and that I may have crossed a line by pushing so hard.

Now I am left wondering if I did the right thing by standing up for my child, or if I went too far and created unnecessary conflict in the process.
