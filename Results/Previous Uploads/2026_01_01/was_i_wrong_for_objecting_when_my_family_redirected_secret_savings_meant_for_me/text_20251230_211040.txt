Was I Wrong for Objecting When My Family Redirected Secret Savings Meant for Me?

I’m a 31-year-old woman who has been financially on her own since finishing university. I worked nearly full-time while studying, paid off my loans without help, and only recently started putting money aside to buy a small apartment. When I was younger, my guardians were very clear that once I finished secondary school, I needed to stand on my own feet. Because of that, I passed on postgraduate programs I was interested in and focused on surviving and stabilizing myself instead.

My younger cousin, who is 23, has had a very different path. He’s brilliant, genuinely gifted academically, and earned a full merit scholarship to a top public university. He graduated with honors and was recently accepted into a highly selective postgraduate program in a major European city. It’s an incredible opportunity, but the tuition and living costs are steep, even with partial funding and a small stipend.

Last month, my aunt and uncle sat me down and asked if I could contribute about €12,000 to help cover his first year. They said they would put in a similar amount and that he’d take out loans for the rest. I said no. I’ve spent more than a decade building myself up from nothing, and I’m finally close to something resembling security. I didn’t feel able to derail that, especially when I’d been told growing up that there was no safety net for me.

That’s when my aunt snapped and said they would just use my “other savings” instead. I was completely confused. After some pushing, she admitted they had quietly set aside roughly €30,000 years ago in my name, intended for a future engagement or wedding. I had never been told this money existed. Not when I was choosing between degrees, not when I was struggling to pay rent, and not now while I’m trying to buy a home.

When I asked why I’d never been given the chance to use it or even know about it, she said it was always meant for a specific purpose, and since I wasn’t partnered or planning a ceremony, they felt it made sense to give it to someone who “needed it more right now.” I felt blindsided and dismissed. I told them it hurt to learn there had been help available all along, just not for the life choices I made.

My cousin later reached out and said he had no idea the money had been earmarked for me and didn’t ask for it. He understands why I’m upset, but he’s focused on school and assumed everyone was on the same page.

I’m proud of him, and I know this isn’t his fault. Still, I can’t shake the feeling that I’m being penalized for being practical, independent, and quiet about my struggles, while decisions about my future were made without me even being in the room.
