Was I Wrong for Refusing to Drive My Friend Across Town for Ingredients the Day Before a Wedding?

One of my friends, Emily, has been trying to start a wedding cake business. She has been promoting herself online for a while, even though she has very little formal training. When potential clients asked whether she had completed professional baking courses, she told them she had. Later, she admitted to me that she had never actually taken any of those courses but felt people would be more likely to hire her if they believed she had.

She has made a handful of small cakes so far. In my opinion, they are still very much beginner-level and not quite what I would expect for a wedding, but everyone has to start somewhere and I assumed she was trying to build experience.

Earlier this year, our mutual friend Megan got engaged and started planning her wedding. Emily immediately volunteered herself to make the wedding cake. It was not really a discussion. She more or less announced that she would be doing it and insisted she would only charge for ingredients. Megan tends to avoid conflict and eventually agreed.

Emily was excited because she saw it as an opportunity to add a real wedding cake to her portfolio and use it to help launch her business.

The problem started the day before the wedding.

That evening, Emily contacted me and asked if I could drive her to another city to buy several ingredients she suddenly realized she needed for the cake. She knew I had specifically taken the day off work before the wedding.

The reason she asked me was because, although she has a driver's license and owns a car, she refuses to drive outside our local area because it makes her nervous.

I told her I couldn't do it. I had taken the day off for a reason. I still needed to collect a gift, finish some last-minute errands, get my hair done, and prepare everything for the wedding. I also admitted I was surprised that, with less than twenty-four hours before the event, she still did not have all the ingredients she needed.

Instead of accepting that answer, she became irritated and asked why I had not finished all my own preparations earlier. I pointed out that I did not need to. I had deliberately set aside the entire day to take care of those things.

She then told me that if I did not drive her, the wedding cake might be ruined.

I apologized but stood my ground. From my perspective, she had known about this wedding for months and had personally pushed to be the one responsible for the cake. It did not seem reasonable to make her lack of planning my emergency.

The next day, I completed my errands while Emily ended up taking a train to get the ingredients herself.

That evening, several of us gathered before the wedding festivities. The moment Emily saw me, she looked annoyed and immediately launched into a story about how difficult her day had been. She complained about walking to the station, waiting for trains, carrying ingredients back, and how much time it had taken away from finishing the cake.

She made it sound as though I had forced her into an impossible situation.

Since then, she has been telling people that I refused to help make sure the bride's wedding cake was not ruined.

That part is what has me second-guessing myself.

I know I was not responsible for buying ingredients for someone else's project. At the same time, this was a wedding, and if the cake had failed, it would have affected the bride more than anyone else.

Part of me wonders whether I should have put my own plans on hold and helped for the sake of making sure the wedding went smoothly, even if the situation was entirely caused by Emily's poor planning.

Was I wrong for refusing to drive her and prioritizing my own preparations instead?
