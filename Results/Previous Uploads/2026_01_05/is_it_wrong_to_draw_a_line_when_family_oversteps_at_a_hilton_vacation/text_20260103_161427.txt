Is It Wrong to Draw a Line When Family Oversteps at a Hilton Vacation?

For the holidays, I booked a luxury suite at the Hilton Grand Resort in Orlando. It was a rare promotional deal—two connected rooms, enough space for eight people—and my spouse and I reserved it for ourselves, our two kids, and a close family friend with their children. We were genuinely excited because this trip felt like a small escape we’d all been looking forward to for months.

Out of nowhere, my spouse mentioned that her brother and his partner had booked a separate room at the same Hilton using the same promotion the night before we arrived. I didn’t think much of it until later that evening, when my spouse casually told me she had already told them—and another family they were traveling with—that they could come into our suite and use our bathrooms after checking out. This other family isn’t close to us at all; I’ve only met them briefly at a couple of kids’ birthday parties, and their child has a reputation for being unruly.

I tried to calmly explain why this bothered me. I wasn’t upset about my in-laws—I actually enjoy them and wouldn’t mind them using our space at all. What made me uncomfortable was having near-strangers and their kids roaming through a suite we were sharing with our own guests, without any discussion or consent. It felt invasive, unfair to our friends, and stressful before a trip that was supposed to be relaxing. When I said I wished she had talked to me first, the conversation immediately turned cold, and she accused me of being controlling before hanging up.

Now I’m left wondering if I’m really being unreasonable for wanting boundaries respected—especially when it comes to shared space and people I barely know—or if it’s fair to expect a conversation before opening our hotel room to extra guests. Is it wrong to say no to strangers using our Hilton suite?
