Was I wrong for keeping a backyard statue that made a visiting parent uncomfortable?

My partner Lena and I live on the opposite side of the country from my family, so visits are rare. Last winter, my parents decided to fly out and stay with us for about two weeks, making it one of the longest stretches of time we’d spent together in years. In our backyard, we have a plastic statue of a folk saint that Lena feels a strong personal connection to. She created a small outdoor space around it where she leaves offerings and spends time, and it also reminds her of her own mother, who passed away a few years ago and shared an interest in the figure. The saint has a complicated reputation and is often misunderstood due to media portrayals and negative groups that have tried to claim the imagery, but for Lena it represents protection, comfort, and a sense of belonging rather than anything dark or threatening.

A couple of days into my parents’ visit, my mother finally noticed the statue through the kitchen window. She visibly startled, hurried back inside, and later asked if we could remove it or hide it for the duration of their stay because it made her uneasy. I explained that it belonged to Lena and had personal meaning, and that it was simply a decorative statue in our own yard. When my father later asked me again to move it for my mother’s sake, I refused once more. After that, my mother avoided the backyard entirely and made a point of turning away from windows where the statue was visible, clearly distressed by its presence. When my parents were leaving, my father told me he was disappointed and felt I hadn’t cared enough about my mother’s discomfort during their visit, while I felt it was unreasonable to expect us to cover up something meaningful in our own home, especially since we aren’t religious and never raised with strong beliefs around such things. I’m left wondering whether standing my ground was justified, or whether I should have compromised simply because my parents had traveled so far to see us.
