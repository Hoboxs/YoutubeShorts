Would I Be Wrong for Telling My Neighbor to Stop Contacting Me Directly?

I’m a 29-year-old woman living alone in a condo complex in Nevada. A few days ago, I took my dog outside for an evening walk when a man I had never met before approached me in the parking area. He looked to be in his mid-50s and immediately started asking whether I lived in a specific unit. Instead of answering, I asked why he wanted to know.

He repeated the question several times before finally explaining that his elderly father, who is in his late 80s, lives in the building and had been complaining about noise coming from above his unit during the middle of the night. According to him, the property office had advised them to contact local authorities if the disturbances continued, but they wanted to speak to the resident first.

I was confused because I had just returned from a six-day trip the night before. When I told him that, he admitted he wasn’t sure exactly when the noise had happened. He only knew that his father believed it was coming from directly overhead and had been disrupting his sleep.

I asked what kind of noise was being reported, but he couldn’t really answer. He said it sounded like furniture moving or people walking around late at night. I pointed out that if anyone heard something concerning, they could simply knock on my door. He replied that his father wouldn’t feel comfortable doing that in the middle of the night.

While we were talking, I noticed an older man standing nearby watching the conversation, which made the interaction feel even more uncomfortable.

The man then started making assumptions about my personal life, including suggesting that I lived with my partner, which isn’t true. He also mentioned that he had already spoken with another resident in the building because they weren’t certain where the noise was coming from. After that, he brought up complaints about a smell he believed might be coming from my unit. I immediately told him he was mistaken.

The whole conversation felt strange because the complaints were vague and seemed to be based on guesses rather than anything specific.

The only incident I can think of that may have caused noise happened before my trip. A friend and I had a disagreement that became louder than it should have for a brief period before they left. It lasted only a few minutes, but I can acknowledge that it may have disturbed someone nearby. If that was what they heard, I understand being frustrated and I’m willing to apologize for it.

What bothered me was the way the complaint was handled. Instead of receiving a note, a message through management, or a knock on the door, I was approached outside by a stranger asking where I lived and accusing me of several things at once. The entire interaction felt intimidating.

Now I’m considering leaving a note explaining that I’m sorry for any disturbance but would prefer that future concerns be directed through management or local authorities rather than having people approach me personally. Since the complaint appears to be based on a single incident rather than an ongoing problem, I feel uncomfortable being confronted this way again.

Would I be wrong for setting that boundary?
