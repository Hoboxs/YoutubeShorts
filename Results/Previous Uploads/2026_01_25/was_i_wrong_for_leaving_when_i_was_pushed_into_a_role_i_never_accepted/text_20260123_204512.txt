Was I Wrong for Leaving When I Was Pushed Into a Role I Never Accepted?

I’m a woman in my early twenties who has never enjoyed being around children, and that has always been something I’ve been open about with the people close to me. I don’t find childcare fulfilling, I don’t feel comfortable being responsible for young kids, and it’s simply not something I want as part of my life right now. That said, someone in my family, an older relative in her thirties, has a young child, and while I care about that child and want her to be safe and happy, my affection has never meant I wanted to act as a substitute caregiver.

Several months ago, that relative began asking me for small favors. At first, it was framed as occasional help, staying with the child briefly while she ran errands or covering short gaps in her schedule. I didn’t enjoy it, but I agreed a few times because I felt put on the spot and didn’t want to cause tension. Over time, those small favors quietly turned into an expectation. I was suddenly watching the child for long stretches, multiple times a week, handling meals, bath time, bedtime routines, and constant supervision. What bothered me most was that no one asked anymore; it was assumed I would comply, and if I hesitated or said no, I was met with anger or accusations.

Every attempt I made to set boundaries was brushed aside. I was told I was young, that my time wasn’t as important, that living under the same roof meant I owed this help. Guilt was used constantly, with reminders about “family” and how I should be grateful or supportive. When I expressed how drained and unhappy I felt, I was labeled selfish, and after hearing that enough times, I started to believe it. I felt stuck in a role I never agreed to and exhausted by the pressure, but I kept going because it seemed easier than fighting everyone.

Eventually, I planned a short getaway with friends, something modest but meaningful to me, and it was arranged and paid for well in advance. For the first time in months, I felt like I had something to look forward to that was just mine. When I shared this plan, instead of support, I was met with immediate hostility. My relative demanded to know what she was supposed to do about her child, as if my plans were irrelevant. I reminded her calmly that I had never committed to being her primary support, and that I had my own life.

That’s when everything exploded. I was accused of abandoning her, of being irresponsible, and of choosing myself over obligations that were never mine to begin with. I was told to cancel my plans and stay behind, as though my time and autonomy didn’t matter. When I refused, the argument escalated into insults and name-calling, and other family members quickly took her side, insisting I needed to “step up” and stop being difficult.

At that point, I felt completely overwhelmed and emotionally worn down. I knew if I stayed, I would either break down or say something I couldn’t take back. So I packed a small bag and went to stay with my partner for a few days, simply to get space and breathe. That decision only made things worse. I was accused of running away, of prioritizing a romantic relationship and friendships over my own family, and of proving that I couldn’t be depended on.

Now I’m left carrying a heavy sense of guilt, especially toward the child, even though I know logically that she isn’t my responsibility. I keep questioning whether leaving was wrong, whether standing up for myself crossed a line, and whether protecting my own well-being really makes me the bad person in this situation.
