Was I Wrong for Not Forcing Myself to Eat an Overpriced Dinner I Didn’t Choose?

During a trip to another city to visit my partner and his relatives, he decided that my final night should be celebrated with a big family dinner. He picked a trendy all-you-can-eat grill-and-sushi spot called FireKoi Table, a place he raved about, and invited four of his relatives along without really asking how I felt about it. The deal was one of those fixed-price menus where you cook everything yourself, and anything left on the table costs extra. I’ve never been a big red-meat person; I mostly eat poultry and seafood and avoid certain meats altogether. Still, I didn’t complain and ordered what I knew I’d actually finish: grilled chicken, shrimp, rice, and vegetables.

While we were cooking, my partner kept loading the grill with beef cuts and several plates of meat I don’t normally eat. I stuck to my food and finished everything I ordered. When he offered me beef, I tried a bit to be polite. When he pushed a different meat, I hesitated, but the look he gave me made it feel like refusing wasn’t an option, so I forced down a small piece. He even tried to pass more of it off as something else, and that’s when I finally said I was full and stopped.

From that moment on, his energy completely changed. The drive back was silent, tense, and uncomfortable. Then he snapped, telling me he’d spent nearly three hundred dollars on dinner and that all I’d eaten was “a few bites of cheap food.” He accused me of being ungrateful and wasting his money, even though I hadn’t ordered the expensive items, hadn’t asked for the restaurant, and hadn’t left anything uneaten on my plate.

I paid attention to what I ordered, finished every bite, and never asked for more than I could handle. He chose the place, he invited the group, and he ordered most of the food that inflated the bill. Still, somehow, I was the problem for not eating things I never wanted in the first place.
