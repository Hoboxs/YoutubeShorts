Was I Wrong After My Friend Fried Her PC With the Device She Took From My Bag?

I’m a 20-year-old student living in a shared townhouse in Halifax, and I keep a special device in my backpack that looks like a simple thumb drive but isn’t. It’s a voltage-discharge stick disguised as a USB, something I carry because my family has a long history of prying into anything left unattended. To make sure I never confuse it with my normal storage drives, I marked it with a tiny red mask sticker. It’s harmless as long as no one plugs it in. That’s the entire point.

Yesterday, I had to leave campus suddenly, so I asked a friend of mine—she’s 21—to keep an eye on my bag until I could pick it up later. When I realized I wasn’t making it back before the buildings closed, I asked her to take it to her place for the night. She agreed without hesitation.

What she didn’t mention until this morning was that once she got home, she decided to explore my bag. She found the red-mask drive, assumed it was my regular one, and out of pure curiosity plugged it directly into her custom gaming setup. The device did exactly what it’s engineered to do: it discharged instantly, and her computer went silent. By the time she had it inspected, the power unit and motherboard were both beyond repair.

Now she’s furious with me, saying I’m irresponsible for carrying something so dangerous around and insisting I need to replace her equipment. But she also openly admitted she was rummaging through my things without permission—and that she plugged in a device she knew didn’t belong to her simply to see what I “had on it.”

I feel awful that her computer is ruined, but I can’t shake the fact that she handled something she wasn’t meant to touch and used it in the one way it’s designed never to be used.

So I’m left wondering: was I genuinely at fault for keeping the device in my own bag, or is she upset because she ignored every boundary and paid the price for her own curiosity?
