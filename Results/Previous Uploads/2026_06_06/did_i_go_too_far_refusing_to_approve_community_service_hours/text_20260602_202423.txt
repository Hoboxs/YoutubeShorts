Did I Go Too Far Refusing to Approve Community Service Hours?

A few weekends ago, I helped coordinate a neighborhood riverbank restoration project in a suburban area outside Portland. The event ran from 8:00 a.m. until noon, and many local high school students attended because they needed community service hours to satisfy graduation requirements. The process was straightforward: participants checked in when they arrived, helped with the cleanup, and at the end of the event I verified the number of hours they had actually worked.

One student, whom I'll call Ethan, arrived with his father around 8:20. He signed the attendance sheet, grabbed a safety vest and collection bucket, took a few pictures near the registration tent, and walked toward the work area. For a little while I assumed he was participating with one of the crews scattered throughout the park.

As the morning went on, however, I noticed I never saw him with any of the teams returning their collected debris. Since I was busy organizing supplies and tracking volunteers, I didn't spend time searching for him. I simply assumed he might be working somewhere farther away.

Around 11:45, just as everyone was packing up and turning in equipment, Ethan suddenly reappeared carrying a smoothie and chatting casually with his father. His dad immediately handed me a school verification form and asked me to confirm four hours of volunteer service.

I hesitated and explained that I couldn't honestly do that. From what I had personally observed, Ethan had been present for less than an hour. I told them I would be comfortable approving one hour, possibly a little less, but not the full four.

His father became visibly irritated and insisted that Ethan had been helping along a different section of the river trail all morning. I replied that if one of the crew supervisors could confirm that, I would gladly adjust the hours. Unfortunately, none of the supervisors remembered seeing him.

At that point, his father lowered his voice and explained that Ethan urgently needed the service hours for school and could face consequences if he came up short. I told him I understood the situation, but I wasn't comfortable certifying work that I couldn't verify.

The conversation quickly became tense. His father accused me of embarrassing his son over a technicality. In reality, there were only a handful of volunteers still nearby, and I never criticized Ethan personally. I simply explained that signing off on hours someone didn't complete would defeat the purpose of the program.

In the end, I approved one hour of service and returned the paperwork. Ethan barely looked up from his phone during the entire exchange, while his father left clearly frustrated.

Since then, I've wondered whether I handled the situation too rigidly. Part of me feels bad because the student may now have to find another volunteer opportunity. On the other hand, community service requirements seem meaningless if people can collect credit for hours they didn't actually work.

Was I wrong for refusing to sign off on the full four hours?
