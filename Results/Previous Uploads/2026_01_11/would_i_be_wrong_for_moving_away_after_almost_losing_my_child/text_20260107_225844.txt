Would I Be Wrong for Moving Away After Almost Losing My Child?

After a terrifying birth that nearly killed both me and my newborn daughter, my life was split into a clear before and after. I’m a 27-year-old woman, married to my 29-year-old husband, and we welcomed our first child after a labor so traumatic that doctors later told us how close it came to tragedy. She was born silent and motionless, and for several agonizing moments, no one knew if she would survive. That experience changed everything about how I see family, safety, and support.

My husband and I grew up in completely different regions of the country. After university, he relocated across the map to live near my relatives. We currently rent a small place about twenty minutes from my side of the family, but despite the distance, they’ve been largely absent. My mother, who lives nearby, has stopped by only a handful of times since the birth. Each visit is brief—just long enough to snap photos for social media—before she disappears again. My father, on the other hand, is very present, but our history is complicated. Growing up, he was controlling, emotionally harsh, and manipulative, and while he dotes on my daughter now, those old wounds never fully healed.

By contrast, my husband’s relatives live roughly twelve hours away and somehow manage to show up more for us than anyone close by. When we visit them, the difference is overwhelming. Meals appear without being asked for. Laundry is quietly done. We’re encouraged to sleep, shower, step outside, and breathe. Babysitters are offered so we can feel like a couple again instead of two exhausted people barely holding it together. When we spend time with my family, the experience is the opposite—hands immediately grab for the baby, no one asks how we’re doing, and help is nonexistent.

Financially, where we live now feels like a dead end. The cost of living is crushing, and homeownership feels like a fantasy rather than a goal. The area near my husband’s family is more affordable, has better schools, and offers the kind of long-term stability we desperately want for our daughter. So we quietly started applying for jobs there, hoping for a fresh start surrounded by people who actually show up for us.

That’s when everything exploded. Somehow my father found out about our plans and accused us of “stealing” his granddaughter. He said if we moved, he would step back from our lives entirely. What hurt the most wasn’t just the threat—it was the realization that he seemed more devastated about losing access to my child than about losing his own daughter. My mother echoed similar guilt-laden comments, questioning why we’d even consider leaving when they’re “right here,” despite rarely making the effort to be involved.

I don’t want to hurt anyone or cut ties out of spite. I know my father genuinely loves his granddaughter, and part of me feels guilty for even considering a move. But I also know what it’s like to nearly die, to watch my child almost slip away, and to realize how fragile everything is. I want a life where my family is supported, stable, and surrounded by people who don’t just want photos, but actually want to help us survive.

So am I really wrong for wanting to relocate to a place where we can finally feel safe, supported, and hopeful about the future?
