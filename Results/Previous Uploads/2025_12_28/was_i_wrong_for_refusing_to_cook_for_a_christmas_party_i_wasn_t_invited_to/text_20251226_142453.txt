Was I Wrong for Refusing to Cook for a Christmas Party I Wasn’t Invited To?

I’m a teenage girl who genuinely loves baking and cooking, and over the past few years it’s become the way I show care for people around me. I often make treats just because, or help prepare meals for family gatherings since I have more free time than most of the adults. Everyone knows this about me, and it’s kind of become “my thing,” especially during holidays.

This year, I was told that Christmas wouldn’t be spent all together like usual. I was disappointed, but I accepted it. A few days later, a relative called and casually asked what dishes I was planning to make for Christmas night. I was confused, because no one had mentioned a party to me. She explained that she was hosting a get-together and that my parent had already told her I would be cooking for everyone.

I didn’t mind helping at first, since I enjoy it and there was still time to plan. But then the conversation shifted. I mentioned getting ready beforehand, and she told me it was an “adults-only” evening. When I asked for clarification, she listed several cousins close to my age who were invited. I realized I was the only one excluded. The explanation given was that I was “not mature enough” to attend late events.

That hurt more than I expected. I felt embarrassed and used — mature enough to provide free food for a crowd, but not mature enough to actually be included. I ended the call and spoke to my parent, who didn’t see a problem with the arrangement and insisted the decision was final. When I said I wouldn’t cook if I wasn’t welcome, the argument escalated. I was told I was being selfish, dramatic, and that if I didn’t help, everyone else would be inconvenienced.

At that point, I was overwhelmed and emotional. I suggested alternatives like ordering food or buying ready-made options, but those ideas were dismissed. Eventually, another parent offered for me to spend Christmas with them instead, somewhere I would actually be wanted. I agreed, which meant I wouldn’t even be nearby to help anymore.

Now the family gathering is apparently scrambling because they had been counting on me for food, and I’m being painted as the reason things are stressful. From their perspective, I’m refusing out of spite. From mine, I just didn’t want to spend Christmas working for a party I wasn’t allowed to attend.

So was I wrong for saying no?
