Would I Be Wrong to Skip Thanksgiving Over My Family’s Rules?

My partner and I usually go to my extended family’s Thanksgiving—my parents, siblings, and their kids. There are about 25 people in total, but it’s just the three of us, including our six-month-old baby.

This year, my brother said he’d handle the turkey and ham because he bought a new smoker. He spent around $450 on meat and told everyone else to bring sides. I offered to make stuffing.

The next day, my sister sent a group chat message saying every “family unit” had to spend at least $150 on food—or chip in to cover my brother’s meat. It felt sudden, controlling, and like a strict rule rather than a suggestion.

Later, she escalated it, basically issuing an ultimatum:

Option 1: bring enough food for the whole group
Option 2: pay $150 to my brother for meat
Option 3: pay $300 toward dad’s heating bill

I was shocked and frustrated. It felt like my participation in Thanksgiving was being turned into a financial test. In response, I texted: Option 4—don’t come.

I love my family, but this year it feels like a ridiculous money trap. Should I just stay home and avoid the stress, or would that make me the bad guy?
