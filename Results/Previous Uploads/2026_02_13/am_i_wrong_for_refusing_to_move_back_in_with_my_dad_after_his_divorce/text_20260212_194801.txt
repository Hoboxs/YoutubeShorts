Am I wrong for refusing to move back in with my dad after his divorce?

My mom passed away when I was an infant, so I don’t remember her. Her older sister stepped up in a huge way and became my constant. I basically grew up with her as my safe person. When I was little, she lived nearby and I saw her all the time. When I was around eight, she relocated for work, and after that I traveled to see her often—long weekends, school breaks, and we split summers. She always made the effort to keep me close.

When I was twelve, my dad remarried a woman named Karen. She had kids of her own. From the beginning, they pushed this idea that Karen was now my “new mom” and that I didn’t need to keep visiting my aunt so much anymore. It felt forced and uncomfortable. Not long after the wedding, my dad gave my bedroom—the bigger one—to Karen’s kids and expected me to move into a smaller shared space. I refused. Around the same time, they also made it harder for me to travel, saying I couldn’t fly alone anymore, which meant my aunt had to either drive hours to get me or pay to fly out and escort me back.

It became pretty clear to me that I wasn’t the priority anymore. I felt like I was being squeezed into their new family instead of still being his daughter. A few months after the wedding, I made the decision to move in with my aunt full-time. It wasn’t dramatic; it just felt obvious. I was happier, more stable, and actually felt wanted there.

Now I’m sixteen. My dad and Karen are divorcing because she cheated, and he recently found out that the younger kids he thought were his aren’t biologically related to him. He called me, crying, saying he misses me and asking me to come back home because he “has no one left.” He says I’m his only real family now.

The thing is, I’ve built a life here. My aunt has been steady and supportive in ways my dad just wasn’t. I love my school, I have solid friends, and we live close to several good universities, so I may not even need to move far for college. My aunt is also seeing someone who treats us both kindly—he comes over on weekends, cooks amazing food, and is even helping me fix up a car. It feels like a real, healthy environment.

My dad and his relatives are saying I’m being cold and selfish. They argue that he’s still my father and that he needs me now more than ever. But I can’t shake the feeling that when he had to choose, he didn’t choose me. And his divorce doesn’t suddenly erase the way I was pushed aside before.

I don’t plan on changing my mind, but part of me wonders: does refusing to move back make me heartless?
