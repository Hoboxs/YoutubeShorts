Was I wrong for refusing to report my cycle to my dad?

When I was twelve, my father decided that I needed to inform him and my older brother every single month when I started my period. We were a household of six at the time—three girls and two boys—and my mother. He claimed that as the “men of the house,” he and my brother had a right to know when I would be “more emotional.” I remember standing in the kitchen of our suburban Ohio home, mortified, trying to explain that it felt private. He shut that down immediately and told me it was disrespectful to keep that kind of information from him. According to him, it was about consideration. According to me, it felt humiliating.

I didn’t comply consistently, which led to repeated lectures about transparency and family communication. My mother never enforced it outright, but she sided with him philosophically, insisting that keeping the peace sometimes meant sharing things we’d rather not. Years have passed since then, and I now live in another state, working as a graphic designer, far from that house and those rules. But the issue still lingers whenever it comes up in conversation. My father maintains he was justified. My mother still says it would have been “the thoughtful thing to do.”

I can’t shake the feeling that it was an overstep. I wasn’t asking for help managing a medical condition. I wasn’t hiding dangerous behavior. I just didn’t want to announce something deeply personal to the men in my family so they could supposedly brace themselves for my mood. It made me feel like my body was public property, like my privacy ranked below their comfort. I’ve tried to let it go, but every time it’s framed as me being stubborn or dramatic, I question myself all over again. Was it really unreasonable to want that boundary?
