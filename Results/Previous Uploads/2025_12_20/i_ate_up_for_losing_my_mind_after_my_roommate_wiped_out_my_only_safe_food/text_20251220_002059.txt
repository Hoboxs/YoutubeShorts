AITA for losing my mind after my roommate wiped out my only safe food?

I’m in my mid-20s, working long, exhausting shifts, and food is already a struggle for me. I only have a handful of meals I can reliably eat, and when I find one that feels “right,” I plan my entire day around it. I’ve made this clear to my housemate before. I even set a very clear rule: if something has been sitting in the fridge for almost a week, it’s fair game. Anything newer than that is off-limits.

On this particular day, I went completely out of my way between shifts to grab a lemon chicken dish from my favorite spot across town. It wasn’t cheap, it wasn’t convenient, and I was already exhausted, but I was genuinely excited to eat it later. I ate a small portion, packed up more than half, labeled it, and put it in the fridge before rushing back to work. I spent the rest of my shift thinking about coming home to that meal.

When I finally got back late that night, I immediately noticed something was off. One of my two-day-old snacks was sitting out on my housemate’s desk, half-eaten. I said something, reminding them I was planning to eat it the next day, and they brushed me off, saying it was “old anyway.” That’s when I opened the fridge and realized the lemon chicken was completely gone.

When I asked about it, they casually told me they ate it. No warning. No message. No hesitation. This was despite the fact that there was still plenty of other food in the fridge they’d already helped themselves to earlier. I tried explaining that I had just bought that meal, that I’d been saving it after a brutal shift, and that I now didn’t have dinner at all. I was met with a shrug.

Later, after I stepped out to cool down, I got a text saying they’d “replace it sometime.” No apology. No acknowledgment of how upsetting it was. Just a vague promise, while I stood there hungry, tired, and furious, staring at an empty fridge where the one thing I could actually eat had been.

Now the house feels tense, and I’m being treated like I overreacted—but I genuinely feel like something important was crossed.
