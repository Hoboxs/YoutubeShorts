Is It Wrong to Refuse to Pay to Attend a Friend’s Birthday Party?

I am a woman in my early twenties, and one of my closest friends, whom I’ve known since we were teenagers, is about to turn another year older. Her birthday falls during a major holiday week, and because of that, she hasn’t really had a proper birthday celebration in several years. The last time she tried, only a handful of people showed up despite her putting in a lot of effort and spending her own money on food and planning. After that experience, she distanced herself from some people in her life and eventually formed a new social circle that treats her much better and shows up for her consistently.

This year, she feels like she’s entering a new chapter of her life and wants her birthday to reflect that fresh start. She told me she planned to go all out, even though it isn’t a milestone age. She wants catered food from her favorite spot, decorations with a fantasy ocean theme inspired by a costume she recently made, and even a live entertainer she really loves who performs at private events. I was fully supportive of the idea at first and encouraged her to celebrate in a big way because I know how much it means to her.

That changed when she broke down the costs. The performer alone would cost several hundred dollars, the food would add a few hundred more depending on the guest list, and while she planned to save money on decorations by making them herself or reusing items, the total would still land close to four figures. Knowing her, I’m confident the party would be beautiful and well organized, because when she hosts something, she truly goes all in.

The issue came up when she told me she wanted to charge guests an entry fee, around twenty dollars per person, in order to help cover the cost of the entertainer. She asked for my honest opinion. I’ve always been very direct with her, and she’s explicitly told me in the past that she wants blunt honesty from me, even if it’s uncomfortable. So I told her flat out that I thought it was a terrible idea. From my perspective, people generally expect birthday invitations to be free, and any money guests spend should go toward a gift, not toward funding the event itself.

She reacted very strongly, called me names, and asked me to leave before things escalated further. Later, her mother reached out to me and said that whatever I said had deeply upset her and that I should apologize. I don’t feel like I was trying to hurt her; she asked for my opinion and I gave it honestly. Now I’m questioning whether my honesty crossed a line or if I was simply being realistic about what guests would be willing to do.
