Was I wrong for refusing to share my vacation space?

I am a woman in my early forties who recently went through a difficult separation after more than a decade of marriage. It took time, therapy, and a lot of effort to feel like myself again, but I finally reached a place where I feel strong, independent, and genuinely happy. As part of that, I decided I did not want to give up the parts of life I enjoyed before, including traveling. So I planned a getaway with a few close friends.

I invited five women, and three were able to join. One friend, who I will call Lauren, declined at the time because she already had other trips planned and limited time off. That was completely fine. I moved forward with the plans, found a rental house with four bedrooms so each of us could have our own space, and we booked everything. Flights were arranged, rooms were assigned, and we were all looking forward to it.

A month before the trip, Lauren called and said one of her other vacations had been canceled, so she was now free to join us. I was happy she wanted to come and immediately offered to help her find a nearby place to stay, either another rental or a hotel close to ours. That is when things took an unexpected turn. She became upset and asked why she could not stay with us. I explained that every room was already taken, something she already knew since I had shared all the details while planning.

She then suggested that I give up my private room so we could share one of the larger rooms together. I told her honestly that I did not want to do that. After spending many years sharing a bedroom in my marriage, I have grown used to having my own space again, and it has become important to me. The other women felt the same way, especially since many of them rarely get time away from their families.

Instead of accepting that, she reached out to the others directly to ask if they would share their rooms. Each of them declined and offered the same solution I had, which was for her to stay nearby so we could all still spend time together during the trip. Despite that, she has now stopped speaking to all of us.

What makes this harder is that she is someone who often travels alone because she prefers not to compromise with others. She had her own room planned for the trip that fell through, so it is not as though we are asking something unusual of her. Still, I cannot ignore the tension this has created. Part of me feels guilty that I am not more upset about her reaction, but another part of me feels like I have worked too hard to regain my sense of peace to give it up now.

I keep wondering if I should have just given in to avoid conflict, or if it is reasonable to hold onto the boundaries I set from the start, even if it costs me a friendship.
