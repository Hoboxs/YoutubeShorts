Was I too harsh telling her she isn’t ready for a child?

I am a woman in my late fifties, and I make it a point to stay close with my family. About once a month, I meet up with my daughters and their partners for a meal or something fun. One of my daughters in law, who I will call Megan, and I have always had a decent relationship. We are not extremely close, but we have gotten along well over the years.

Megan has never learned how to drive. She struggles with anxiety and has avoided it for a long time. Because of that, my son usually drives her everywhere she needs to go. When he cannot, I often step in to help. Public transportation in our area is not very reliable, and rides through car services are expensive, which makes things more complicated for them financially.

Recently, we all met for dinner like we usually do. During the meal, Megan casually shared that she and my son were planning to have a baby. I was surprised, and I must have shown it on my face without realizing. I did not say anything at the table, and the conversation moved on.

After dinner, I drove her home since my son was not available. On the way, she kept asking me why I reacted the way I did earlier. At first, I tried to brush it off, but she kept pressing me again and again. Eventually, I told her the truth. I said I did not think she was ready to have a child, mainly because she cannot drive and depends heavily on others to get around.

I pointed out that I was the one driving her home at that very moment, and asked what would happen if my son was away for work. I questioned how she would handle emergencies or even routine things like doctor visits with a child. I mentioned that relying on me or expensive rides did not seem like a solid plan, especially given their financial situation.

What followed was a heated argument. She told me I was being disrespectful and that I did not see her as capable or independent. I stood by what I said, explaining that my concerns were practical, not personal. Still, she was deeply upset and called me hurtful before getting out of the car.

Later, my son called and asked me to apologize, not just for how I said it, but also to support their decision to start a family. I am struggling with that request because I genuinely do not believe it is a good idea right now. At the same time, I can see how my words may have hurt her.

Now I am left wondering if I crossed a line by being so direct, or if I was right to speak honestly when she kept pushing for an answer.
