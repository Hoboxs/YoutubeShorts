Was I Wrong to Clear Out My Bathroom and Trigger a Meltdown?

I share a flat in Brighton with three other renters, and each bedroom is paired with its own bathroom. Since the fourth room is empty this year, the bathroom beside my room is assigned solely to me. Unfortunately, my newest flatmate and her partner seem to think that “assigned” simply means “free for anyone who wanders by.” They use it constantly, never touch the soap, and leave the extractor fan running for days. The fan doesn’t have a timer, overheats easily, and our letting agent warned us months ago that it’s a genuine fire risk when left on too long.

Every time I’ve contacted her, it’s been about something hazardous or inconsiderate—talking loudly beneath my window at 1 a.m., turning the radiators up to the mid-30s, leaving the dryer full of lint, or letting cigarette smoke drift through the corridor. I didn’t want to nag, but she left me no choice.

Before leaving for a two-week winter break, I taped a small note to the mirror reminding her to please turn the fan off and wash her hands if she used the bathroom. At dawn the next morning, she switched everything off… only to turn the fan back on immediately afterward without touching the sink. That was the moment I decided I wasn’t leaving any of my belongings within reach while I was away.

So I packed up everything—skincare, towels, cleaning sprays, and yes, the toilet paper. It’s my bathroom, stocked with my supplies, and I wasn’t going to pay for her and her boyfriend to burn through everything while ignoring the most basic hygiene.

A few hours after my train left the station, my phone lit up with frantic messages. Apparently they had walked into my bathroom, found the empty holder, and spiraled. They accused me of being childish, of making the flat “uncomfortable,” and of acting like I “owned the place.” The irony? That bathroom actually is assigned to me; they were never supposed to be using it at all.

Now they’re stomping around telling our other flatmate that I’ve “sabotaged the shared atmosphere,” while I’m sitting here wondering if I simply protected my space from two people who were never entitled to it in the first place.

So was I wrong to clear out my own bathroom before leaving or is this just the reaction of two people who lost access to something they should never have been using?
