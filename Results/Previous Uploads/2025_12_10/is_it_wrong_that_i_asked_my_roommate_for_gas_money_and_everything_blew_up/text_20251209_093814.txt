Is It Wrong That I Asked My Roommate for Gas Money—and Everything Blew Up?

A few months ago, I was competing in a two-week shooting competition. It required a 35-minute drive each way, every single day—over an hour of driving on top of physically exhausting long sessions at the range. My roommate was also competing, but his car had completely given out, so he rode with me every day.

At first, I didn’t think much of it. But halfway through the first week, I started noticing how quickly my gas tank was draining. The cost wasn’t small, and it genuinely wasn’t something I had budgeted for—not daily, not at that distance.

One evening, after an especially long day in the heat, I finally asked him if he could spot ten dollars to help cover gas. Just ten. Without even looking up from his phone, he said, flat as stone:
“Not gonna happen.”

It wasn’t just the refusal—it was the dismissiveness. I was tired, stressed, and suddenly very aware that I was footing the entire bill for both of us. I told him that if he couldn’t chip in even a little, he’d need to find a different ride.

The next day after shooting, some of our mutual friends called me over. Apparently, he had gone straight to them and told them everything—except, of course, the part about refusing to help at all. They took his side immediately, telling me I was being “unreasonable,” that I needed to be a “good roommate,” that asking for gas money was out of line.

It felt like being ambushed. I didn’t have the energy for a conflict, and the pressure from everyone made me cave. I apologized—even though a part of me hated doing it—and let him ride with me for the rest of the competition.

But now that time has passed, I still can’t shake the feeling that something about the whole situation was very one-sided. I was the only one driving, the only one paying, and the only one asked to “be a good roommate.”

So I keep wondering, was I really out of line for asking for ten dollars in gas money, or was I just expected to carry the full burden without saying a word?
