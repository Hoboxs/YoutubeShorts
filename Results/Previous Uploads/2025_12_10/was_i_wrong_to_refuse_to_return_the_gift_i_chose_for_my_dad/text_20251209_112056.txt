Was I Wrong to Refuse to Return the Gift I Chose for My Dad?

I’m 24, and every winter my family in Dorset has a tradition where we all send out holiday lists filled with links and notes so no one buys duplicates. My mother manages the list and updates everyone whenever something gets purchased. I had finished all my shopping weeks before the lists went out, and one of the things I bought for my father was a cast-iron griddle set he’s been hinting at for ages.

My grandmother—who I’ll call Marjorie—has always been intense. She’s in her late seventies, sharp as a blade, but extremely controlling. She was raised in an era where chaos was normal, and it shows in how she micromanages everything from who carves the roast to which chairs people sit in. My father still freezes up when she raises her voice. When she’s cheerful she’s genuinely funny, but those moments are rare.

After the lists went out, my mother marked the griddle set as “already purchased” so no one else would buy it. She sent the usual update to our group chat. A few days later, Marjorie called my father and demanded to speak to my mother. She proudly announced she had bought him the griddle set—plus two other items that were also already marked as purchased. My mother gently reminded her that all three gifts had been crossed off days earlier. Marjorie immediately insisted that she had never received “any such notification” and said that everyone else should return their items instead. Returning hers, apparently, was not an option.

I decided I am not returning the gift I chose for my dad. I picked it myself, long before any lists went out, and I know he’ll love it. Since I live with my parents, he’ll get mine weeks before Marjorie even arrives for the holiday visit. I’m aware that when she finds out, she’ll likely retreat into silence for months, making a dramatic production out of being “wronged,” because she does this every time anyone challenges her.

The part that makes me hesitate is that she really does struggle with technology. She calls my father constantly for help adjusting settings, opening files, even finding her email. It is genuinely possible she never saw the updated list. And since returning things takes her much longer now, I understand why she’d prefer not to deal with that.

But at the same time, she refuses to acknowledge anyone else’s effort. She simply expects the world to reorganize itself around her choices. That’s what she has always done.

Was keeping my gift the right choice, or did I just light the match for another one of her dramatic storms?
