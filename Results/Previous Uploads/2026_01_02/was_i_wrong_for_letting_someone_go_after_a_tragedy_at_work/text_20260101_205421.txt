Was I Wrong for Letting Someone Go After a Tragedy at Work?

I’m a senior manager at a mid-sized tech company, and earlier this year one of our junior team members experienced a devastating loss. He’s a 23-year-old analyst who had been with the company for just under a year, and his performance before everything happened was excellent. He was well-liked, consistently hit his targets, and there had already been internal conversations about fast-tracking him into a higher role within the next several months. His position pays well for someone early in their career, just under six figures with bonuses included, and the expectations attached to it are high.

In early spring, both of his parents were killed in a sudden accident. It was shocking and awful, and as a company we tried to respond with compassion. We immediately approved several weeks of fully paid leave and told him to take the time he needed. After about a month away, he returned to work. Unfortunately, once he was back, his performance dropped sharply. He was disengaged, missing deadlines, and no longer completing the core parts of his role. This went on for a few weeks, and it became a consistent concern across the leadership team.

After multiple internal discussions, management decided that the role simply couldn’t remain unfilled or underperforming for an extended period. We felt that realistically, he would need a long time to recover emotionally, and the business couldn’t absorb that gap. We called him into a meeting at the end of the week and told him we were letting him go. He reacted very calmly at first, then lashed out verbally, told us off, gathered his things, and left without another word.

I was taken aback by his reaction and felt it crossed a line, even given the circumstances. When I later explained the situation to my partner, they were furious on his behalf and told me that what we did was cruel and heartless, regardless of business needs.

I understand that the loss he experienced was unimaginable, but I also feel responsible for the health and performance of the team as a whole, so I’m left wondering if we truly handled this wrong or if this was an unfortunate but necessary decision?
