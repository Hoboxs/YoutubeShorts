Was I Wrong for Refusing to Give My Brother a House Key After Years of Boundary Issues?

I’m a 39-year-old woman, and my younger brother is 35. For most of our adult lives, I’ve been considered the responsible sibling. I have a stable career as an accountant, own my home, and generally live a pretty structured life. My brother, on the other hand, always seems to be navigating one emergency after another. Between frequent job changes, financial problems, and relationship drama, there’s almost always some new crisis unfolding.

I care about him deeply, but I’ve reached the point where seeing his name pop up on my phone late at night immediately makes me wonder what problem I’m about to be asked to solve.

About eight months ago, he found himself in a difficult situation. He was locked out of his apartment building after midnight during a snowstorm and had his two young sons with him. When he called, I immediately drove across town, picked them up, and let them stay at my house for the night. I didn’t hesitate because they’re family.

After that incident, however, he began asking for a spare key to my house “for emergencies.”

I declined.

It wasn’t because I was worried he would intentionally damage anything. The problem was that his definition of an emergency has always been much broader than mine. Over the years, I had loaned him things that somehow turned into ongoing obligations. He once borrowed my SUV for a weekend and returned it nearly empty with several parking tickets I ended up dealing with. A short-term loan of money remained unpaid for over a year until I finally stopped asking about it.

Individually, none of those situations were catastrophic, but together they painted a very clear picture. Boundaries that start small tend to expand quickly.

A month ago, I came home after running errands and found my brother sitting on my front steps with his sons. Apparently there had been another issue at his apartment, and he needed somewhere to stay for the evening. I welcomed them inside without argument and helped get everyone settled.

Later that night, while we were having dinner, he brought up the spare key again.

I told him no.

I repeated that I wasn’t comfortable giving out a key to my home. Instead of dropping it, he kept pushing. Eventually he asked what I thought would actually happen if he had one.

By that point, I was frustrated.

I told him the truth. I said I believed my house would slowly become his backup residence whenever life became complicated. I reminded him about the vehicle, the unpaid loan, the pet-sitting situation, and several other examples where temporary favors had turned into long-term responsibilities.

The room went quiet.

He asked whether I really thought that little of him. I said it wasn’t about thinking poorly of him; it was about patterns of behavior I had personally experienced.

To my surprise, he became emotional. He didn’t get angry or start shouting. Instead, he just sat there and cried. His boys were nearby and heard part of the conversation, which immediately made me feel uncomfortable.

The next day, my father called and said that while he understood my concerns, I had embarrassed my brother during a period when he was already struggling. He argued that I could have found a gentler way to refuse rather than listing every reason I didn’t trust him. According to him, being honest wasn’t the problem—it was the delivery.

Since then, my brother has barely spoken to me.

Some relatives think I was completely justified because a spare key grants access to someone’s home and requires a high level of trust. Others believe I should have simply given him the key and addressed any issues later if they arose. To me, that seems backwards because by then the boundary would already be gone.

I keep going back and forth. I know the examples I brought up were real and directly related to why I said no. At the same time, I wonder if I could have handled the conversation differently, especially with his children sitting nearby.

Was I wrong for refusing to give him a key and explaining the reasons when he wouldn’t accept my answer?
