Did I Cross a Line by Buying a House Beside Someone From My Past?

I am a man in my early thirties, and during university I was extremely close with a woman my age. We spent nearly every day together and trusted each other completely, until our friendship ended badly. She took part of my academic work and presented it as her own, which led to me being questioned by faculty and senior staff. The situation was stressful and humiliating, and although it eventually resolved, our friendship never recovered.

While we were still close, we talked a lot about where we grew up. She told me she was from a coastal town I will call Harborwick, and I mentioned that I was from a different seaside place, Bayford. Hearing she was from Harborwick stuck with me, because my mother used to vacation there every year, and when I was nineteen I scattered her ashes there after she passed away from cancer. That town has always held deep emotional meaning for me.

Harborwick is a very popular and expensive area in England, the kind of place where family homes often cost close to a million pounds and rarely come up for sale. Out of curiosity, I checked a property site one day and found a house listed for a surprisingly low price. It needed extensive renovation, which explained the cost, but the location was perfect. It was steps from the sea, close enough for me to run along the beach daily for my mental health, a short walk from where my mother’s ashes rest, and within easy distance of a train station that connects to London, where I commute a couple of days a week for work.

I bought the house and did not even step inside for the first month because contractors were handling major repairs. After the new year, I started doing the remaining work myself. A few weeks later, a delivery driver asked if I could take in a package for my neighbor, who was not home. When I saw the name on the label, I immediately recognized it because of its unusual spelling. That was when I realized I lived next door to my former friend and her parents. I declined to accept the package.

Soon after, she discovered I was her neighbor. She showed up at my door visibly angry, yelling loudly enough that several neighbors came outside to see what was happening. Since then, I have heard through relatives that some people think I am in the wrong for creating tension on the street and that I should consider moving.

The truth is, I did not choose this house because of her. I chose it because it is affordable, meaningful, and ideal for my life and wellbeing. I have moved on from what happened years ago, and I do not believe I should give up a home that means so much to me simply because someone else is uncomfortable living nearby.
