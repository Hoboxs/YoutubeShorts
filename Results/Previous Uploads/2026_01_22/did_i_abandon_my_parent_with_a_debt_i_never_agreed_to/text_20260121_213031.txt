Did I Abandon My Parent With a Debt I Never Agreed To?

I am a twenty year old man who has been paying on a car loan tied to my parent since I was seventeen. For my birthday back then, my mother gave me what she called a gift, a brand new sedan that I assumed was reasonably priced because her then partner, who claimed to be good with money, handled the dealership paperwork. I later learned the car was financed for nearly double its original value, with no money down and a high interest rate because my mother was unemployed and had poor credit at the time. The monthly payment was enormous, but the agreement was that I would split it with her partner. That arrangement lasted only a few months before I was left covering the full payment alone, which I have done ever since.

When I was nineteen, I tried to move the loan fully into my name, but the bank explained that refinancing would increase the total cost significantly, so we both agreed not to do it. I have since moved out, share an apartment, and am struggling more with expenses. After some advice from people around me, I started looking for a more affordable vehicle and found a lightly used compact car that costs far less than what I am still paying off. I told my mother I planned to buy it and that she could decide whether to sell the original car herself, which immediately led to a blowup. She insisted the car was my responsibility because it was a gift, claimed she did me a favor by keeping the loan in her name, and demanded I empty my savings to continue paying for it.

What makes this harder is that she is newly divorced, unemployed, and supporting two younger children. I feel torn between believing I should not be financially trapped by a decision I did not make or understand at seventeen, and feeling guilty for leaving her with a massive debt during a very vulnerable time in her life. I am trying to figure out whether protecting myself financially makes me heartless, or whether it is reasonable to finally step away from a burden that never should have been mine to begin with.
