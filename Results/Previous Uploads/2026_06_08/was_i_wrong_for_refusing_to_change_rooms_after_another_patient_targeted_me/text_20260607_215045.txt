Was I Wrong for Refusing to Change Rooms After Another Patient Targeted Me?

I’m a 22-year-old woman, and a few months ago I ended up spending nearly a week in a medical center in Glasgow, Scotland, after developing complications from a severe infection. My condition wasn’t considered life-threatening, but the doctors wanted to keep me under observation and administer treatment through an IV.

Because the ward was busy, I was assigned to a shared room with a woman named Lorraine who looked to be somewhere in her early sixties. During the first day, everything seemed perfectly normal. We exchanged a few polite comments, and I assumed we would simply coexist until one of us was discharged.

Things changed the following morning.

Lorraine started making passive-aggressive remarks every time she saw me scrolling through my phone. She repeatedly commented that I was “far too energetic” to be in a hospital and implied that younger people exaggerated their medical issues. At first I ignored it, figuring she was having a rough time herself and didn’t want to create tension.

A couple of days later, my cousin stopped by after work to visit me. We spent about half an hour talking quietly about family updates and some ridiculous stories from home. For the first time since being admitted, I actually laughed and felt somewhat normal. The moment he left, Lorraine exploded.

She accused me of treating the ward like a social club and claimed I had been inconsiderate to everyone around me. I apologized if our conversation had carried farther than I realized, but she kept going. According to her, I was constantly disturbing people with phone calls, messages, and visitors. The reality was that I only spoke with worried relatives a few times each day and had received a total of two visitors during my entire stay.

That evening, a charge nurse approached my bed and asked if I would be willing to relocate to another room because Lorraine had submitted several complaints about me throughout the week. I was surprised because no member of staff had previously told me I was violating any rules.

I asked why I was the one being moved when I hadn’t received any warnings and had already unpacked my belongings, medications, books, and personal items. The nurse admitted that the request was mostly being made to avoid further conflict and “help maintain harmony” on the ward.

I declined.

I explained that I was comfortable where I was, I had done nothing wrong as far as I knew, and it didn’t seem fair to inconvenience me simply because another patient disliked me. The nurse accepted my answer and left.

When Lorraine realized I wasn’t moving, she became even more upset. She started crying loudly, saying nobody respected her concerns and that people were always choosing younger patients over older ones. The atmosphere became incredibly awkward for the rest of the night.

After I was discharged, I told my aunt about what happened. She thought I should have switched rooms just to avoid the confrontation and make my stay easier. Meanwhile, several friends told me that Lorraine had been hostile from the beginning and that giving in would have rewarded her behavior.

Now I’m wondering if I was being stubborn. Would it have been easier to simply move, or was I justified in refusing to accommodate someone who seemed determined to find fault with me from the moment we met?
