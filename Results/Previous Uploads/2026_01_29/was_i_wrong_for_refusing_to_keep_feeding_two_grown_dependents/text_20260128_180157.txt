Was I wrong for refusing to keep feeding two grown dependents?

I am a twenty year old woman living at home with my family, and I am honestly torn between guilt and relief over what just happened. My father is in his early fifties and, to my knowledge, has never cooked a full meal for himself in his entire life. My mother handles all of the cooking, on top of working and managing most of the household chores. When she gets frustrated or exhausted and expresses that she needs help, my father does not step in. Instead, he mocks her complaints or sulks about how tired he is, which usually ends with her giving up and doing everything herself anyway.

Because of this dynamic, I learned to cook very young. I was around twelve when I became the backup parent in the kitchen. Whenever my mother was sick, overwhelmed, or simply needed a break, I was the one who cooked for everyone. Over the years, this quietly became an expectation rather than a favor. My younger brother, who is thirteen, has grown up assuming that meals will simply appear in front of him. If our mother is not cooking, then I am. He has never seriously been expected to prepare food for himself, even though he is perfectly capable of learning.

My mother does occasionally tell my father and brother that they should cook for themselves, but it never sticks. She caves every single time because it is easier than dealing with their attitudes. As a result, nothing ever changes and the responsibility always slides back onto her or me.

Recently, my mother left town for three days. Before she went, I decided that I was done enabling this pattern. I did not announce it dramatically or start a fight. I simply chose not to step in automatically. I want to be clear that I did not abandon them or set them up to fail. There was already cooked rice and quinoa in the fridge. All they needed to do was make a couple of omelets and some fried potatoes. Extremely basic food that does not require advanced skills or effort.

They managed just fine. No one went hungry. Nothing caught fire. The kitchen was still standing.

Despite this, my father absolutely exploded. He yelled at me and told me I was cruel and claimed I was somehow far worse than my mother. He acted as if I had committed a serious betrayal by not taking over her role. There was no acknowledgment that I had been cooking for years without complaint or that expecting a grown man and a teenager to prepare simple meals was reasonable.

The strangest part is that instead of feeling ashamed, I felt relieved. For the first time, I did not cover for their learned helplessness. I did not sacrifice my time and energy to keep the peace. I simply stepped back and let them handle something that should never have been my responsibility in the first place.

Now I am left questioning myself. Was I genuinely cruel for refusing to cook for them, or was this a necessary boundary that just made them uncomfortable because they are not used to being held accountable?
