Did I go too far telling my sister to stop controlling our father’s grief?

After our mother died nearly six years ago, our family froze in that moment, especially my younger sister, Mara, who was still in high school while I was already living on my own. Our father, Daniel, centered his entire life around protecting her pain, even years later. Every time he tried to date, Mara would spiral—crying, accusing him of erasing our mom, begging him to stop—and each time, he did. Recently, Daniel began seeing a woman named Claire, a divorced teacher with two sons, and for the first time he seemed genuinely alive again. When he suggested spending the holidays together, Mara shut it down, claiming the house was her “safe place” and full of memories that couldn’t be shared. Then I learned—through a family chat—that she planned to move back in after college, a plan she’d never mentioned until Claire entered the picture. Frustrated and exhausted, I accused her of using our mother’s death to control him and acting as if she were the only one who lost her, while the rest of us were expected to stay frozen forever. Daniel told me to stop, Mara left the chat, and later I was labeled heartless for “attacking” her grief. Now my father wants me to apologize, but it feels like once again, his happiness is being sacrificed—and I’m the only one willing to say it out loud.
