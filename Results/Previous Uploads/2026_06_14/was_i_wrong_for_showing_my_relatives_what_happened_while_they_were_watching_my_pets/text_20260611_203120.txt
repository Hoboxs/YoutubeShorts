Was I Wrong for Showing My Relatives What Happened While They Were Watching My Pets?

I’m 34, and earlier this year I went on a 10-day overseas trip. I asked my 35-year-old second cousin to stay at my house and care for my pets. We were very close growing up, but I had distanced myself because of her past substance abuse issues and poor judgment. Recently she seemed more stable, so I decided to trust her.

Before leaving, I explained that one of my cats needs medication on a strict schedule and that all the pets required regular care and fresh water.

On the first night, I checked my security cameras and saw my cousin arrive with her five-year-old son. A few hours later, an unfamiliar man came to my house looking upset that she hadn’t answered his calls. She let him in, and he stayed overnight.

I was uncomfortable because I had never met him, never approved overnight guests, and my pets are nervous around strangers. I was also concerned because her son was there and alcohol had been brought into my home. I messaged her, explained that this crossed a boundary, and she apologized, promising it wouldn’t happen again.

Over the next several days, I noticed through the cameras that she was away from the house for long periods. Then one evening she left after dinner and didn’t return until late the following afternoon, ignoring my calls and messages the entire time.

Worried about my pets, I asked a nearby friend to check on them. They found several water bowls empty and confirmed the animals had not been cared for as instructed.

When my cousin finally responded, I told her I had arranged alternative care and no longer needed her help. I asked her to leave the key and let my friend take over.

The next day, her parents began calling and texting me, saying I had overreacted and treated her unfairly. It was clear they had only heard her version of events.

After repeated criticism, I explained what had happened: the overnight guest, the long absences, the ignored messages, and the neglect of my pets. When they seemed doubtful, I sent them security footage showing the man arriving and leaving.

That changed everything.

My cousin later called me furious, accusing me of humiliating her and turning her parents against her. I told her I hadn’t contacted them first—I only defended myself after they accused me based on an incomplete story.

Now some family members think I should have kept the details private, while others believe I had every right to explain what happened.

Was I wrong for telling them the truth and sharing the footage to defend myself?
