Is Saying No at Work a Crime Now?

I’m in my early thirties and work at a modest-sized firm with a few dozen employees, the kind of place where everyone pretends to know everyone else but most interactions are shallow and transactional. Last week, a senior administrator named Marlene took it upon herself to sweep through the office announcing that we were all “pitching in” for a birthday surprise for a woman named Talia, someone from a completely different division whose face I recognize only because we’ve passed each other near the coffee machine a handful of times. We’ve never collaborated, never spoken beyond polite nods, and certainly never shared anything resembling a relationship. Marlene declared the contribution would be thirty-five dollars per person for a gift card and an elaborate dessert, as if this were a foregone conclusion rather than a request, and when I didn’t immediately respond, she appeared at my desk later that afternoon with a tight smile and asked if I was participating. I calmly told her I was opting out, explaining that I don’t usually contribute to group gifts for people I don’t actually work with or know, which seemed reasonable to me. Her expression shifted instantly, surprise curdling into mild disapproval, and she brushed it off with a pointed, “It’s not a lot of money,” before walking away. Since then, the air around me has changed—conversations go quiet when I approach, smiles feel forced, and one colleague even joked loudly that I was “the holdout,” as if I’d committed some kind of social betrayal. Now I’m left wondering whether I unknowingly violated some sacred workplace ritual or if refusing to buy affection for a near-stranger has somehow branded me as difficult or stingy, even though I genuinely believe generosity should be voluntary and personal, not enforced through side-eyes and whispered judgments.
