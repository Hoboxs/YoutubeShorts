Did I Seriously Become the Office Villain for Not Decorating My Own Home?

My manager is brand new, barely a few months into the role, and ever since she took over, the vibe at work has shifted from professional to strangely performative. A couple of weeks ago, she announced what she called mandatory overtime and corralled our entire remote team into a two-hour video call at the end of the day. By the time I logged off, I’d worked nearly thirteen hours straight. The following week, we quietly learned the truth: the overtime had never been required at all. It wasn’t policy. It was pressure. That alone left me exhausted and wary.

Then came the holiday “team bonding” idea. She asked everyone to send in photos of their home decorations so she could turn it into a guessing game during yet another meeting. There was an unspoken expectation that everyone would play along, smile, and perform enthusiasm. The problem is, I don’t decorate. I never have. I don’t enjoy it, I don’t see the point, and I don’t want to buy things just to photograph them and throw them away later. My home is my space, not a prop for workplace morale.

Instead of faking it, I answered honestly. I told her I don’t decorate and left it at that. I didn’t criticize the idea, didn’t make a scene, didn’t demand special treatment. I just stated a fact. Her reaction was immediate and sharp. She told me I was “ruining the game,” as if my refusal to stage a festive backdrop had single-handedly derailed team spirit. The message was clear: I should have pretended, lied, or gone out of my way to perform joy for the sake of appearances.

That’s when it really hit me. After being manipulated into unpaid overtime, now I was being guilted for not decorating my private living space to support a manager’s idea of fun. I work remotely. My job performance has nothing to do with how my home looks. I’m not willing to buy decorations, borrow them, or pull a random image off the internet just to keep the peace.

Now I’m sitting with this uncomfortable feeling that honesty made me a problem, and that not playing along turned me into the villain. All I did was draw a line around my own home and my own time, yet somehow that was framed as selfish and disruptive. It makes me wonder how quickly boundaries become offenses when people expect compliance instead of consent.
