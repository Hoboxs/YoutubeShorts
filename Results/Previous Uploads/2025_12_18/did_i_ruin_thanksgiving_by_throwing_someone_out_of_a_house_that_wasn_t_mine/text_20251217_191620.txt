Did I Ruin Thanksgiving by Throwing Someone Out of a House That Wasn’t Mine?

I went to Thanksgiving with my wife and our 14-year-old daughter to a relative’s house. Also there were my nephew, his boyfriend, and my cousin by marriage, who has a habit of saying offensive things and calling it “just being honest.”

While I was in the kitchen, my daughter pulled me aside and told me my cousin was being “really weird” and making the situation uncomfortable. I went to check and found him loudly making homophobic comments toward my nephew and his boyfriend. Nothing physical, but it was clearly escalating, and tempers were flaring.

I took my cousin outside and told him he needed to leave. He argued that it wasn’t my house and I had no authority, but I stood my ground and didn’t let him back in.

Later, the host demanded to know where he was. When I explained, she exploded, saying I ruined Thanksgiving and that what he said “wasn’t that bad.” She told me, my wife, my daughter, and the couple to leave and said we might be invited back next year “once we learn to behave.”

We left and had a surprisingly nice dinner elsewhere, but since then I’ve been getting messages from family saying I overstepped and had no right to kick anyone out.

Now I’m stuck wondering whether I protected people from a situation getting worse—or if I crossed a line by acting when it wasn’t technically my place.
